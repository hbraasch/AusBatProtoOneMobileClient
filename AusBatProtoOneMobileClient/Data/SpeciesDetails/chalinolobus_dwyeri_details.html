Large-eared Pied Bat
Chalinolobus dwyeri
Ryan, 1966

Description A caricature of a Chalinolobus, with its large ears and curly wattles, the pied bat has uniform glossy black fur on the back. The belly is washed with brown, usually with a band of bright white fur along the sides of the body where the wing membranes join. These white lateral stripes converge in the pubic region to form a V-shape. Ears are very large and easily touch when pressed together above the head. The lobe on the lower outer edge of the ear near the corner of the mouth is well developed. A second lobe of skin along the side of the lower lip is also well developed.

Habitat This species is commonly associated with a variety of habitats in proximity to roosting areas in cliff rock faces, boulder piles and gorges. These habitats most commonly encompass woodlands, forests and associated grasslands. They also occur in sub-alpine woodland, along the edge of rainforest tracts and wet sclerophyll forest and Callitris dominated forest.

In the Sydney basin they have been found to be common in areas of high fertility soils in wet sclerophyll forests along the edges of sandstone escarpments and often forage along forest-grassland border areas, with fewer calls recorded in open areas or in the forest. They also forage along creeks or moist drainage gullies containing riparian vegetation.

Roost Habits These bats roost in caves, overhangs, crevices in cliffs and abandoned mines, individually or in colonies of up to 40, often clustered in indentations in the ceiling. They prefer the twilight areas of the caves not far from the entrance. Males have been found during the winter in deep torpor roosting alone in abandoned mines and one juvenile male was found overwintering in an abandoned fairy martin nest.

In mid-November a maternity colony of 40 females and newborn twins was found in a sandstone cave near Coonabarabran. The bats were clustered into two separate cracks in the twilight area in the cave ceiling. The temperature ranged from 15.5 to 18.5 degrees and 51 to 76% humidity.
 
There is no evidence to suggest that these bats use tree hollows.

Diet, Flight and Foraging These bats are insectivorous, but no details are known. They fly relatively slowly with rapid but shallow wing beats. Their flight is direct and moderately manoeuvrable. I (S.C.) have watched them fly low along a creek bed, but they also fly at mid-canopy level 6 to 10 m above the ground. Radio-tracking studies in the Blue mountains have shown that females have larger foraging areas than males, with both sexes showing high loyalty to preferred foraging areas even after several months. Females foraged further from the roost than males. Forage with a slow fluttery flight along forest edges at mid to upper canopy height and around tree crowns occasionally diving down to capture prey. Males had a smaller foraging range (71Ha) than females (113Ha). Most bats travelled less than 1 km from the roost (one individual was recorded traveling 2.5 km).

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls

Characteristic frequency between 21.5 and 25.5 kHz (n = 14). Curved with up sweeping, down sweeping or no tail. Pulses often alternate in frequency and can easily be distinguished from other species by the combination of the low frequency and the distinct pattern of alternation often present in search phase calls. Data indicates that this species does not noticeably vary its call throughout its range.
