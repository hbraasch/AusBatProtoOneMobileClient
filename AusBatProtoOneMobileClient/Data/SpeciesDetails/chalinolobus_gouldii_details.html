Gould's Wattled Bat
Chalinolobus gouldii
(Gray, 1838)


Description An amazingly adaptable bat that is found in the full spectrum of the Australian climatic zones. It has a delightful habit of ‘murmuring’ when held in the hand. The fur is brown on the back and belly with contrasting dark blackish fur on the head and shoulders. Specimens from northern Australia may be almost totally black. The muzzle is short, the ears are short and broad. The back edge of the ear extends to form a large lobe of skin (wattle) at the corner of the mouth and a secondary long narrow lobe along the lower lip. It shows variation in body size throughout its range. Individuals from northern Australia are smaller than those from southern Australia and those from eastern Australia are smaller than western individuals. There is no significant difference in size between males and females.

Habitat This ubiquitous bat is found in virtually all habitats throughout Australia from alpine regions to tropical rainforests, eucalypt forests and woodlands, deserts, grasslands, agricultural land and urban areas. They do not occur on the treeless plains of the Nullarbor and are very rare in northern tropical savannah woodlands with only a very small number of records from Cape York, Top End and Kimberley.

Roost Habits Gould's Wattled Bats roost most commonly in tree hollows and hollow limbs of living mature trees, particularly river red gums (Eucalyptus camaldulensis) and cypress pines (Callitris). Other recorded roosts include buildings, a tree stump, amongst leaves, in a roll of canvas, and even in the exhaust pipe of a tractor. Females form colonies of 8 to 40 in tree hollows and up to 80 in buildings. Males are usually solitary but have been found sharing a tree hollow roost with several other bat species and feather-tail gliders (Acrobates pygmaeus).

Diet, Flight and Foraging Bugs and moths are the major food items, including Bogong moths in the southern highlands. A wide variety of prey including winged and wingless ants, cockroaches, stoneflies, katydids, field crickets, cicadas, bugs, beetles, flies and caterpillars have been reported from scats and stomach remains. They regularly forage from 5 to 10 kilometres from their roost site and occasionally up to 15 kilometres away. They fly just below or within the lower level of the tree canopy and along the forest edges, creek lines and around isolated paddock trees. They have an agile flight that is faster than that of most other vespertilionids and tend to fly on a fixed horizontal plane with abrupt zigzag changes of course, frequently rolling to near vertical banking angles during horizontal turns. They can fly at speeds of up to 36 km per hour but cruising speed is usually from 11 to 18 kmh.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls

Characteristic frequency is between 25 and 34kHz (n = 122). Usually curved, with a down-sweeping tail or no tail. Consecutive pulses usually alternate in frequency although when ‘cruising’ in open spaces, calls may even out and lose alternation. When long sequences are recorded, some alternation is usually seen, however. Good quality calls should not be confused with any other species.
