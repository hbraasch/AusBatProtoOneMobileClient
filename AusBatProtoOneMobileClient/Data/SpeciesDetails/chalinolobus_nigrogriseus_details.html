Hoary Wattled Bat
Chalinolobus nigrogriseus
(Gould 1856)


Other Names Hoary bat

Description A gentle animal, the hoary Wattled Bat has dark grey to black fur on the back and greyish brown on the belly, with a frosting of white tips to the hairs, giving a hoary appearance (some individuals have no white frosting). The lobe (wattle) at the base of the outer margin of the ear near the corner of the mouth is poorly developed and the secondary lobe is reduced to a thin ribbon of skin along the lower lip. There is an east to west variation in size, and the amount of white frosting to the fur. Western Australian individuals are smaller with considerably more frosting than Queensland bats which may be almost black. The Little Pied Bat, C. picatus, can be distinguished by the presence of a notch in the inner upper incisor.

Roost Habits Hoary Wattled Bats are forest dwellers that roost in small hollows in eucalypt trees. They have also occasionally been found to roost in rock crevices. In Papua New Guinea a maternity colony of several hundred females and their young were found in the roof of a building in November. They can often be seen flying about just after sunset, having left their roost site well before other bat species have emerged.

Habitat They occur in many habitat types, including monsoon forest, tall open forest, open woodland, dry sclerophyll forest, river red gum riparian woodland, littoral rainforest, deciduous vine thickets, coastal scrub, sand dunes, spinifex covered hills, grasslands and floodplains. They fly fast below the canopy level so forests with naturally sparse understorey layers may provide the best habitat.

Diet and Foraging In their diet they show a preference for beetles, ants and moths but they also eat a wide variety of prey including spiders, mantids, earwigs, crickets, grasshoppers, cockroaches, cicadas, bugs, flies and lacewings. Hoary Wattled Bats commonly forage along water courses and in swampy areas in northern Australia, and are also found around isolated dams and waterholes in drier areas. They have been observed at twilight foraging along the edge of forest, along the tops of forest crowns and circling isolated trees about 5 m from the crown edge. Their foraging flight is moderately fast with speeds of up to 34 km/hr recorded. They are agile and respond to prey at a range of 3 to 5 m making frequent course changes in response to evasive manoeuvres by the prey.

Vocalisations and Vocalisations and Echolocation calls s 

Characteristic frequency 36 to 40 kHz. Usually with no tail or occasionally up sweeping tail. The calls may be difficult to distinguish from Scotorepens greyii where these species overlap in range. Reinhold et al. (2001) reports that C. nigrogriseus have relatively longer characteristic and tail sections, usually 2/3 or more of the total pulse which, in conjunction with the lack of up-sweeping tail, may help to differentiate them.
