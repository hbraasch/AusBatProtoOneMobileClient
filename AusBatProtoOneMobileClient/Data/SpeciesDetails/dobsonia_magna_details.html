Naked-backed Fruit Bat
Dobsonia magna
(Thomas, 1905)


Other Names Dobsonia moluccensis magna, Dobsonia moluccense, Bare-backed Fruit Bat.

Taxonomy Although Dobsonia magna is larger, there is some taxonomic uncertainty about its relationship to D. moluccensis, recent DNA analysis using limited samples was inconclusive.

Description This large and distinctive Fruit Bat differs from Flying Foxes in its unusual wings that join along the mid-line of the back rather than along the sides of the body. The furless wings give the impression of a hairless back. The back is fully furred under the wings where deep pockets have been formed on either side of the body. The fur on the shoulders and head is black brown. The belly is a lighter grey brown and sparsely furred. There is a short tail. Claws on the wings and feet are white and there is no claw on the second digit of the wing. Males are usually heavier than females and can weigh up to 600 g, maximum weight for females is 525 g. Forearms Although a large and strong bat they are surprisingly gentle when handled. The unusual wing shape causes this bat to have a characteristic sound in flight, a hollow 'pock-pock-pock', caused by the air compressing in the spaces under the wing membrane pockets on its back.

The eyes are large and create an obvious eye-shine in a cave or in the forest when viewed with a spotlight. There are two pair of musk producing sebaceous glands. One pair on the shoulders near the wing junction and another pair that runs from the base of the ears towards the corner of the mouth. These glands become more prominent in males during the breeding season. 

Roost Habits Naked-backed Fruit Bats live in small colonies of less than 100 In Australia, although they are found in colonies of many thousands in New Guinea. They usually roost in twilight conditions in caves, under boulder piles, and in disused mines and concrete bunkers, as well as in dark rainforest thickets and large tree hollows. A colony of 13 that I (S.C.) came across near Iron Range was roosting in a pile of large granite boulders 2 to 3 m above the ground. Initially the bats were hanging from the walls alone or in pairs but when disturbed they flew off and on return, roosted together clumped into a single tight mass.

They are highly manoeuvrable and as an individual approaches a group of roosting bats they are able to hover, repeatedly reverse direction and then slowly fly in to land by swinging their feet upwards to grab onto the roost. If disturbed at the roost they can be very vocal, otherwise there are only occasional aggressive calls between individuals coupled with “wing boxing” as they attempt to displace each other from mates or favoured roost positions.

Their ability to dissipate body heat is up to 180% of that expected for a mammal of its body weight. This is due to the large area of wing membrane that acts as a radiator, allowing them to shed heat easily in flight and while at the roost. Predators include large Amethystine pythons (Morelia amethistina). Large fur-dwelling batflies are common (particularly in large cave colonies). They include Megastrebla parvior and M. gigantea. In New Guinea, terrestrial leaches often hang from cave ceilings and have been observed to drop onto the bats to feed.

Habitat Found from sea level to 2,770 m elevation. Primary and secondary rainforest, gallery forest and tropical woodlands. They are rare in dry woodlands and savanna.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  They feed on a variety of native fruits (particularly Ficus, Terminalia, and Callophylum), and eucalypt blossom, the Northern Bloodwood, Corymbia polycarpa is one of its favourite food trees. They also enjoy domesticated fruit such as Banana, Musa spp, Papaya, Carica papaya and Football fruit, Pangium edule. Like Flying Foxes they tend to follow water courses when commuting between roost and feeding areas but unlike them they forage alone and are silent at night.
