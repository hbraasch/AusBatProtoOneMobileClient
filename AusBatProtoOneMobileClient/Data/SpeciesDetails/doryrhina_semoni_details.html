Semon's Leaf-nosed Bat
Doryrhina semoni
(Matschie, 1903)


Other Names Hipposideros semoni, Semon’s Horseshoe Bat, Greater Wart-nosed Horseshoe Bat

Taxonomy Recently moved to the genus Doryrhina from Hipposideros, based on a revision of African bats by Foley et al. 2017

Description A delicate, finely built species with a forearm of 43.1 to 50.4 mm and weight of 5.7 to 8.7 grams. The ears are very long and narrow, with an acute narrow point, and lacking an anti-tragus. The fur is long, dark smoky grey to brown and has a ruffled appearance. It is slightly lighter on the belly. No orange or rufous morphs are known. A distinctive feature is the square-shaped nose-leaf with a large wart like projection in the centre of the nose leaf and the small secondary wart on the posterior nose-leaf. There are two lateral leaflets, the outer one joins the posterior leaf. There are relatively large and sculpted foliations around the deep nostrils. The posterior leaf is thin and divided into four of cells.

Roost Habits Semon's Leaf-nosed Bat has been found roosting in some strange places. These include an oven, a clothes closet, a picture rail in an unoccupied house and even hanging from the door handle of a car. They have more commonly been reported roosting in caves and in tree hollows. I have caught this bat in a small granite boulder cave with a roost microclimate no different to that of the surrounding rainforest. They have been caught in groups of up to 8 individuals in mist nets in rainforest. Three were recorded near Cooktown, roosting alone in different rock fissures in limestone tower karst. Roosts were less than 7 m from the entrance in the twilight zone. They usually roost alone but a colony of 10 individuals has been recorded. Most roost microclimates are very similar to outside conditions but in one complex cave system they were found in small dead-end passages that were warm and humid. They are uncommon and restricted to just a few areas but are consistently captured in low numbers.

Habitat Tropical rainforest, gallery forest, monsoon forest, wet sclerophyll forest and open savanna woodland.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They are known to eat moths. The presence of huntsman spider and beetle remains under two cave roosts suggest that these may form part of their diet. To catch huntsman spiders the bats would need to be partial gleaners, taking prey from surfaces such as rock faces, tree trunks or from the ground. Bats released in the rainforest flew low, within a metre of the ground. Like other species of Hipposideros, the flight was relatively slow and manoeuvrable as they carefully negotiated their way through the undergrowth. Bats observed exiting a cave roost after sunset, were observed to fly without echolocating in the moonlight, relying on vision and memory to negotiate obstacles. We have watched one flying low (20 cm) to the ground along a semi-dry, rocky waterway, circling a small pool and flying back and forth along the rock bank. It flew very slowly, much like the Dusky Leaf-nosed Bat, Hipposideros ater and tended to stay very close to surfaces such as rocks and shrubs and rarely moved far into open space. It roosted on a small tree, constantly rotating its body back and forth with its ears twitching as it echolocated and searched its surroundings. After 10 minutes or so it moved on. We have seen them fly in surprisingly strong wind by keeping close to the ground and staying in eddies. They negotiate mist nets with ease.

Echolocation The echolocation calls of this species are unusual in that they show a sexual dimorphism, with males and females producing calls of different frequencies. Males call at 94 kHz and females call at 74 kHz.
