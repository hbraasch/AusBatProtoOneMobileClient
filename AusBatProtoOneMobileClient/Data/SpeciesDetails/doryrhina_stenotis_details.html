Northern Leaf-nosed Bat
Doryrhina stenotis
(Thomas, 1913)


Other Names Hipposideros stenotis, Northern Horseshoe Bat, Lesser Wart-nosed Horseshoe Bat

Taxonomy Recently moved to the genus Doryrhina from Hipposideros, based on a revision of African bats by Foley et al. 2017.

Description The Northern Leaf-nosed Bat, Doryrhina stenotis is a delicate and finely built species with a forearm length of 41.9 to 45.6 mm and weight of 4.6 to 6.7 grams. The ears are long and sharply pointed, extending well beyond the fur. This is a feature that can be clearly seen from a distance when the bat is roosting. The ears lack an anti-tragus. The fur on the back is grey to brown and slightly lighter on the belly. Their fur is very long and fine with a white base to the hairs, giving a rather ruffled and untidy appearance. No orange or rufous morphs are known. A distinctive feature is the square-shaped nose-leaf with a large wart like projection in the centre of the nose leaf and the small secondary wart on the posterior nose-leaf. There are two lateral leaflets, the outer one joins the posterior leaf. There are sculpted foliations around the deep nostrils. The posterior leaf is thin and divided into four of cells. It is similar to Semon's Leaf-nosed Bat, D. semoni, of eastern Queensland, which is slightly larger (forearm length 43.1 to 50.5 mm) and has larger wart-like projections on the nose leaf.

Roost Habits Northern Leaf-nosed Bats roost in shallow sandstone caves, overhangs, boulder piles, road culverts and disused mines. They have not been found in limestone caves despite extensive searches in the Northern Territory. Individuals roost alone, usually in well separated pairs or in small (maternity) colonies of 6–12 bats. Roost microclimates are characterised by low humidity and bats roost close (less than 50 m) to the entrance, often in the twilight zone (recorded temperature of 27oC and 46% humidity). Most caves are small, shallow overhangs or splits in sandstone cliffs. These bats hang by their toes from the ceiling, usually away from the walls and do not cluster. when there are two at the roost they are at least 30 cm apart. They are easily overlooked. I have known one to wait quietly at its roost, 50 cm off the ground, in a boulder pile, as I crawled past it only centimetres away. I saw it for the first time as I turned around to leave the cave. In mines they appear to be more alert and slip away quickly and quietly hugging the rock wall when disturbed, usually flying outside rather than entering the deeper, more humid areas of the mine. They are seldom caught in mist nets or bat traps in the open. These bats could be confused with the Dusky Leaf-nosed Bat, H. ater, but their long pointed ears are noticeable at the roost and they roost in different microclimates (H. ater requiring humid roosts); while on the wing they are larger, with a more direct flight. Despite extensive bat surveys in places such as Kakadu National Park there are few records of this species and it is thought to be genuinely rare.

Habitat The Northern Leaf-nosed Bats is closely associated with steep hills, rocky outcrops and escarpments. It has been recorded in monsoon thickets, deciduous vine thickets, tall open forest, open eucalypt woodland (Darwin woollybutt Eucalyptus miniata, Darwin box E. tectifica, Darwin stringybark E. tetradonta, Round-leaved Bloodwood E. latifolia with grassland or shrubland understories, floodplains, open grassland, and spinifex covered hills. In the Kimberley, it has been caught along creeks and pools fringed by stands of Broad leaved Paperbark, Melaleuca leucadendra, Silver tea-tree, M. argentea, Pandanus spp. and Rock fig Ficus platypoda. Their distribution is influenced by the availability of suitable roost sites. On the Kimberley Islands they were found roosting in sandstone caves in savanna woodland and escarpment with hummock grass, Ficus and Calytrix.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls

Characteristic frequency is 102-106 kHz, constant frequency. Duration is 10.2 – 21.1 ms.
