Western False Pipistrelle
Falsistrellus mackenziei
(Kitchener, Caputi and Jones, 1986)


Other NamesWestern Falsistrelle, Pipistrellus tasmaniensis.

Description The largest vespertilionid in Western Australia, this is a large and robust bat. The fur on the back is dark brown with a light cinnamon belly. The snout is naked forward of the eyes; the slender ears extend well above the head and overlap when pressed together. The ears are triangular with a rounded tip that curves backward and there is a characteristic notch on the outer margin near the tips. The tragus is more than half the length of the ear, with a moderate sized lobe at the rear base and narrowing to a slightly rounded tip. The penis is hairy. This bat has two pairs of upper incisors, but the outer pair are very small and hard to see. There is a gap between the upper canines and the incisor teeth.

Habitat Western False Pipistrelles are restricted to areas in or adjacent to stands of mature forest, usually in pre-senescent types of old growth. It is estimated that 12 - 15% of the south-western forests remain as old growth. Typically these bats are found in wet sclerophyll forest dominated by Karri (Eucalyptus diversicolor), and in the high rainfall zones of the Jarrah (E. marginata) and Tuart (E. gomphocephala) dry sclerophyll forests. They have been recorded in mixed Tuart-Jarrah tall woodlands on the adjacent coastal plains. Marri (E. calophylla) and Tingle (Eucalyptus jacksonii) forest and adjoining open woodland. Sheoak (Casuarina heugeliana) and Peppermint (Agonis flexuosa) trees are often co-dominant at their collection localities. They have also been captured in Banksia woodlands on the Swan coastal plain.

Roost Habits These gregarious bats roost in hollow trees, branches and stumps. They have been found in colonies of 5 to 30. A group of five bats released in the forest gathered around the canopy of one tree before settling together on a dead branch to roost. The sexes segregate for roosting at least during much of the spring and early summer when the young are being reared. A colony of about 30 individuals roosted in a Karri tree hollow about 15 m above the ground.

Diet, Flight and Foraging These bats have been caught in mist nets set 4 to 8 meters above the ground. Their flight is reported as fast and direct with occasional abrupt turns as they forage in the air space amongst the massive tree trunks between the understory and high canopy. They catch airborne insect prey. They have also been caught in bat traps and mist nets set at normal height along forest tracks (S. Churchill, B. Thomson, pers. comm).

This species is the largest of the air-superiority insectivore bats that inhabit the south-west forests of Western Australia. Because of their large size (20 gm) they are likely to need a particularly productive habitat to survive hard times. This bat had a historical range that takes in the south-west corner of the State from Cape Leeuwin, past Albany and north up the Darling Range to the latitude of Perth. Recent examination of its preferred climate has shown a marked reduction in rainfall and relative humidity over the last 30 years. The data and climate modeling projections suggest that F. mackenziei may soon retreat from the areas to the south and east of Perth and perhaps even from the coastal plains between Mandurah and Cape Leeuwin. Clearly this species is under threat from global warming and the ongoing drying of the South-west corner of Western Australia that is projected by current climate models. 

Vocalisation and Echolocation Their echolocation calls are of a relatively low frequency. They have a steep, frequency modulated call, with the lowest frequency of 30 kHz, peak energy between 32 and 24 kHz and a duration of about 12 milliseconds.
