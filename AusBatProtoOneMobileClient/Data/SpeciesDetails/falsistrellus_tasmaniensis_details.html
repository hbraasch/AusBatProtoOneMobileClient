Eastern False Pipistrelle
Falsistrellus tasmaniensis
(Gould, 1858)

Other Names Eastern Falsistrelle, Tasmanian Pipistrelle, Pipistrellus tasmaniensis

Description This is a large and robust bat. The fur is dark brown to reddish brown on the back with and ventral fur is brown with pale tips. The muzzle is sparsely haired. The slender ears extend well above the head and overlap when pressed together. There is a characteristic notch on the outer margin of the ear near the tip. The tragus is more than half the length of the ear; it has a moderate sized lobe at the rear base and narrows to a slightly rounded tip. The penis is hairy. There are two pairs of upper incisors: the outer pair are very small and hard to see. There is a gap between the upper incisors and the canines. These features help to distinguish it from the greater Broad-nosed Bat, Scoteanax rueppellii. It differs from the western False Pipistrelle, F. mackenziei by being slightly smaller (greatest skull length is 18.3 mm compared to 19.2 mm).

Habitat In Tasmania and on the mainland they are found in wet sclerophyll and coastal mallee. They prefer tall and wet forests where trees are over 20 m high and the understorey is dense. They occur in open forests at lower altitudes. In Queensland they have been captured within tall open forest, sub-tropical riparian rainforest, cool-temperate rainforest and open forest with E. acmenoides and E. tereticornis.

Roost Habits Eastern False Pipistrelles generally roost in hollow trunks of eucalypt trees in colonies of 3 to 80. Colonies are usually almost entirely male or female groups although evenly mixed colonies sometimes occur. They have been recorded roosting in a cave at Jenolan, New South Wales and they are occasionally found in old wooden buildings. In the Australian Alps roost trees were exclusively older smooth-barked eucalypts. Most roosts contained solitary bats, but one contained a colony of 79 males, 1 female and 3 white-striped Free-tailed bats Austronomus australis. Radio tracked individuals changed roosts almost every night, and returned to old roosts on different nights. Roosts on consecutive nights were usually less than 750m apart although one bat moved 3.5 km between roosts. They have a home range of up to 136 ha. A banding study showed a recaptured adult female was recaptured 88 km from its original banding site 3 years earlier. One radio tracked bat was found to move 12 kilometres from where it foraged to where it roosted in a very large tree.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They tend to target the largest available prey items. On the mainland they eat mainly moths, beetles (chaffer, weevil and rove beetles) and some bugs, flies and ants. In Tasmania they eat mainly beetles (75%), moths (10% and bugs (8%). Their flight is swift and direct, within or just below the tree canopy. They tend to fly fast in a fixed horizontal plane with sudden darting changes in course. They hunt in gaps and spaces within the forest and avoid thick rainforest understorey or dense regrowth. They are absent from small patches of remnant forest preferring continuous forest, where they forage along tracks, creeks and rivers. They are also capable of moving through cleared landscapes and have been recorded foraging in open areas.
 
Vocalisation and Echolocation
Characteristic frequency between 35 and 39 kHz (n = 32). Curved and often steep without an up- sweeping tail, occasionally tail down sweeping. Pre- characteristic section often long.
Can be separated from Scotorepens greyii and Scotorepens sp. (Parnaby, 1992) by the lack of up-sweeping tail on most pulses. Easily confused with S. orion and Scoteanax rueppellii, however may be differentiated on length of pre-characteristic section in some calls (Reinhold et al. 2001).
