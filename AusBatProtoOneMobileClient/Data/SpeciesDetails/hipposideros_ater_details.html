Dusky Leaf-nosed Bat
Hipposideros ater
Templeton, 1848

 

Other Names Hipposideros bicolor, Dusky Horseshoe Bat.

Description One of Australia’s most delightful bats, the dusky Leaf-nosed Bat is a small and delicate species. It differs from the fawn Leaf-nosed Bat, H. cervinus, in its small nose-leaf that lacks secondary leaflets. The ears are large, rounded and extend well beyond the top of the head. Fur long and very fluffy making the bat appear much larger than its 4 gm mass. Fur colour is variable, usually pale grey-brown but interestingly in caves where it co-exists with the Orange Diamond-faced Bat, Rhinonicteris aurantia, it takes on the same fur colour as that species, varying from orange to white. The orange colour is likely due to bleaching in the high ammonia atmosphere of the caves.

Roost Habits Dusky Leaf-nosed Bats roost hanging by their toes from the ceiling with their wings and body free from the rock surface. They hang at a distance of at least 10 to 20 cm from other individuals and do not cluster. They roost primarily in relatively deep caves or disused mines but have also been found in small overhangs, splits in escarpments, and boulder piles. The most important feature of the roost is high temperature and humidity. In the Northern Territory their roost microclimate averages 29oC and 92% relative humidity. Within the cave they select the warmest and most humid sites available, showing a strong preference for avons that trap warm air. In large caves they tend to select small side chambers and dead ends, away from other species. Colony sizes vary from solitary bats to groups of 300 but are commonly 10 to 30. A colony of 11 has been recorded roosting in a large tree hollow in rainforest at McIlwraith Range on Cape York Peninsula. In the Top End they often roost in the same caves the Orange Diamond-faced Bat, Rhinonicteris aurantia and have the same fur colour, ranging from orange to yellow to white individuals. Ghost Bats, Macroderma gigas, also commonly share their cave. The small isolated colonies such as those at Cloncurry, Chillagoe, and Blackdown Tablelands in Queensland and the Dulcie Ranges, in central Australia, are dependent upon the presence of caves with unusually high humidity, enabling these delicate bats to live in these arid inland environments. 

Habitat A wide variety of tropical habitats are used including rainforest, monsoon forest, gallery forest, mangroves, Melaleuca swamps, scrubby open forest, mixed open forest, open woodland, vine thickets, savanna woodland and spinifex grasslands.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They are primarily continuous flight foragers, capturing all of their prey while in flight or gleaning it from surfaces. They do not use perch hunting. They fly below the canopy, frequently amongst dense vegetation, in tall grass or close to the ground. Flight observation show that they have a cruising speed of 10 to 12 km per hour but will fly much slower when foraging, usually about 4 km/hr. They are also capable of hovering. Their flight is slow irregular fluttering flight with occasional brief hovers and glides. They are often less than 50 cm above the ground and rarely over 2 m altitude. Several individuals may forage in close proximity where aerial insects are abundant. Their fluttering flight is manoeuvrable but not agile. They use a variety of turns from gentle horizontal turns, banking turns with angles of less than 30o, skidding turns, and open wing-overs. Their slow flight means that they do not travel far from their roost sites to forage. They tend to fly low along walls and leave cave entrances cautiously, one at a time, following contours around rocks, close to the ground and rapidly disappear into the vegetation. This behaviour would help them to avoid predators such as Ghost Bats hunting from the air and tree snakes hanging from cave walls and ceilings. Studies in Queensland have shown them to consume almost entirely moths, 90% of these were eared-moths (Noctuidae). The high frequency echolocation calls of the bats are beyond the moths hearing range enabling the bats to hunt eared-moths without them taking evasive action. In the Top End their diet is less specialized, although moths still made up over 50% of the diet they also eat beetles, termites, grasshoppers, ants, leaf-hoppers, mantis flies, lacewings and mosquitos. They are a delightful companion in areas with high numbers of mosquitos as they will circle you repeatedly, eating numerous mosquitos on each pass until all are gone. They can easily detect monofilament mist nets even in very cluttered habitats and will often flutter along the net until they find a hole large enough to fly through. They don’t always manage to avoid nets; I once found a dead one caught in the web of a golden orb-weaving spider. They can also evade capture from double-banked harp traps, and if caught, can easily escape from the trap bag. Triple-banked traps are more successful. They are very sensitive to dehydration and cannot be kept in captivity for more than a couple of hours. Bat traps must be checked frequently, and the bats released as soon as possible.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls H.ater has a constant frequency search phase call type, much higher than any other bat, 152 to 175kHz. The constant frequency echolocation technique employed by the leaf-nosed bats allows them to detect the speed and direction of very small flying insects with great accuracy.

Notes This is a delicate species that dies readily in captivity and in traps from dehydration and hypothermia. They have a constant frequency echolocation call that varies from 175 kHz in the Kimberley to 152 kHz in Queensland.
