Queensland Diadem Leaf-nosed Bat
Hipposideros diadema
(Geoffroy, 1813)

Other Names Diadem Horseshoe Bat.

Description A formidable bat, this is the largest Leaf-nosed bat in Australia, forearm length averages 81.8 mm (77.5 to 85.1 mm) and weight averages 43.9 g (32.0 to 57.0 g). The fur on the back is a pale grey to light brown (there is also a deep burgundy red form) with a distinct white patch on each shoulder. The ears are of long being broad at the base and acutely pointed. The nose leaf is well developed, with three or four lateral supplementary leaflets, the fourth is small and sometimes rudimentary. There is no frontal sac.

Roost Habits Diadem Leaf-nosed Bats are most commonly found in caves and disused mines, but also in buildings and road culverts. They show a preference for large caves with large chambers and multiple entrances. I have recorded roost microclimates of 25 to 26.5oC and 65 to 80% humidity. They usually hang from the ceiling by their toes, their wings folded at their sides and separated from each other by 20 to 25 cm. A study at Chillagoe caves showed they used 9 of the 18 suitable caves. Colony sizes ranged from 1 to 65, usually less than 10 with many solitary males. In September 1981 I found 2000 of these bats in Gordon’s Mine at Iron Range. At this density the bats roosted close together, across the ceiling and down the wall of a section of passage to within 30 cm of the floor. More recently the colony has ranged from 70 to 250 with 100 being typical. In New Guinea they roost singly or in small groups during much of the year but form large maternity colonies in caves when giving birth and nursing young. Queensland Diadem Leaf-nosed Bats can effectively thermoregulate at temperatures as low as 9oC. but their thermal-neutral zone is at 29 oC to 34 oC. At 29oC their body temperature is 34.9oC and they can drop their body temperature down to 33.0 oC. They do not enter torpor. Their basal metabolic rate is 33.0 cm3 O2-h-1, with a minimal conductance 5.51 cm3 O2-h-1oC. Low basal rates of metabolism are consistent with perch hunting behaviour which is characterized by intermittent flight and long periods of rest, saving considerable energy. They have a basal metabolic rate that averaged 75% of those bats that used continuous flight.

Habitat Diadem Leaf-nosed Bats use a variety of habitats from lowland rainforest, Melaleuca forests, eucalypt woodland, and deciduous vine thicket to open woodland.

Diet, Flight and Foraging These large bats are occasional carnivores. Recent work has found the remains of birds in their scats. Their more usual diet is large hard bodied insects such as beetles and grasshoppers, as well as large moths, bugs, and wasps. A light-tagging study of these bats at Chillagoe showed that they exit from their cave soon after dusk and fly directly to regularly used individual foraging areas usually within 2 to 10 kilometres of the cave. At the foraging area they hunted from perches that were 5 to 15 m above the ground. While hanging from the perch the bat twists continuously from side to side, with ears twitching constantly while echolocating to locate prey. When prey is detected, they drop from the perch, catch the prey with their mouth and fly back to the perch before eating it. If a swarm of insects appear the bats remain on the wing to catch several, temporarily storing them in cheek pockets before returning to the perch. Foraging flights have been observed at the edge of vegetation, in small clearings, and in open woodland. They spend between two and seven hours foraging each night usually in two activity peaks and spent the remainder of the time roosting in caves. Average foraging range was 1 km. They often continue foraging for to up to an hour after first light. They have large cheek pouches for temporarily holding food while they are in flight. They have long wings and a high wing loading like the carnivorous Ghost Bat, Macroderma gigas. They have been recorded killing bats, one killed a Least Blossom Bat, Macroglossus minimus that was caught in a Bat Trap. They are also known to consume other bats in captivity.

Notes They have a formidable set of teeth, strong jaws for crunching beetles and an unpleasant and tenacious disposition when being removed from a mist net. Bites to the fingers are a memorable experience. Heavy gloves are strongly recommended. They readily quieten down in captivity. Jenny Mclean at the Tolga Bat Hospital has cared for a beautiful female (who has a damaged wing) for many years. She is very tame but quite an aristocrat and goes by the name of “Lady Di”
