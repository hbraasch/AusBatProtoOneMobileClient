Arnhem Leaf-nosed Bat
Hipposideros inornatus
McKean, 1970

Other Names Hipposideros diadema inornatus, Diadem Horseshoe Bat (part). Diadem Leaf-nosed Bat (part)

Description A rare bat that is better natured than its cousin, the Queensland Diadem Leaf-nosed Bat, H. diadema. The Arnhem Leaf-nosed Bat is distinctly smaller with a forearm of 68.1 to 73.4 mm and lacks the white fur patches at the shoulders. The fur on the back is pale brown with slightly darker tips and is only slightly paler on the belly. Bright orange individuals have also been recorded.

Roost Habits This species is known from only a few large sandstone caves with multiple entrances. These are characteristically draughty, cool (23 to 25oC and 75 to 85% humidity), and close to water. They have recently been recorded from a large, abandoned mine in Kakadu National Park. In 1989 I (S.C.) located 2 cave colonies. One at Mt Callanan, in Kakadu National Park, where a colony of 14 adults and 8 young were roosting about 25 cm apart in a cool area of the cave, under large boulders, 1 metre above the floor and only a few metres from a small stream running through the cave. In the other colony, at Dinner Creek near Oenpelli up to 15 of these bats roosted in similar conditions in a large sandstone cave. Over a number of years, I (S.C.) have revisited a colony in a sandstone cave with a collapsed roof at Tolmer Falls, in Litchfield National Park. The cave is cool and draughty and has a small spring running through it. This colony roosted 3 m above the ground. It had contained 15 individuals in 1978, 8 in 1984 and none during multiple trips from from 1987 to 1995.

Habitat  Monsoon forest, tall open forest, sandstone monsoon forest, open woodland as well as open heath on the sandstone plateau. Found only in sandstone escarpment areas close to water.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  At Deaf Adder Gorge in Kakadu National Park, a large number of these bats (92) flew through my (S.C.) campsite one evening. The bats were flying along the edge of the creek through thick riverine monsoon forest, and many could be heard chewing and crunching as they flew past. While eating they flew straight with a gliding flight. When not eating they had a fast and zigzagged flight. They flew well below the canopy at between 1 and 3 m above the ground. They were flying fast and moving quickly downstream presumably so they could spread out to forage once outside the confines of the gorge. Many were captured the following evening by placing mist nets in the forest. Stomach contents have been found to consist mostly of beetles (78%), with some moths (20%) and traces of cockroaches and leafhoppers.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls
The largest leaf-nosed bat in the Top End it has a much lower characteristic frequency than the other Leaf-nosed Bats (67.1 to 71.2 kHz). The distance over which calls of this species can be detected is estimated at around 10 m so it is easily recorded with ultrasound detectors.
