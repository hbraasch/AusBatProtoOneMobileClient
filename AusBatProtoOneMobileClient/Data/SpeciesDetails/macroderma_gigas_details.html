Australian Ghost Bat
Macroderma gigas
(Dobson, 1880)

 

Other Names Megaderma gigas, Ghost False Vampire, False Vampire, Australian False Vampire.

Distribution Once distributed across most of inland and northern Australia as far south as the Flinders Ranges in SA. Recorded until 1970 in central Australia but now restricted to tropical northern Australia. Endemic to Australia. Macroderma gigas also appears to be widespread and common on the coastal islands of the Kimberley and Top End although it hasn’t been recorded from the Tiwi Islands.

Description A spectacular bat, this is largest echolocating bat in Australia and one of the largest in the world. It has pale grey or light brown fur with a lighter belly. Individuals tend to be paler, almost white, in more inland populations. The wing membranes are pale cream to brown. It has no tail but retains a full tail membrane. The very large ears are joined above the head and there is a large forked tragus. It has large eyes and a long simple shaped nose-leaf extends along the top of the muzzle.

Roost Habits Ghost Bats roost in shallow sandstone caves along cliff lines, under boulder piles, in deep limestone caves and in abandoned mines. They have a preference in the Top End for caves with roost microclimates of 27 to 29oC and 80% relative humidity. They roost either individually or in colonies of 30 to 200, with one colony of over 1500. Ghost Bats cannot lower their body temperature and enter torpor, so they must find roost sites with temperatures above 23C in order to conserve energy. These bats commonly roost hanging from the ceiling about 25 cm apart but they roost more closely when they have young. The females congregate in maternity roosts, these are usually in larger caves and mines but I have also found small groups of females with young roosting in boulder piles and shallow escarpment overhangs. Females show a strong preference for remaining in the area they were born whereas males tend to move more readily to different areas. When leaving the roost to forage, pregnant females leave the cave first. They are trapped consistently earlier than non-pregnant females and adult males. Ghost Bats are sensitive to roost disturbance, particularly when they have young. The young are sometimes dislodged during rapid take-offs and the adults may abandon the roost site. Cave searches along cliff lines can inadvertently flush individuals out into the daylight where they are taken by birds of prey. Many other bat species have been found roosting in the same caves as Ghost bats but they usually roost in a different part of the cave. Ghost bats share a roost at certain times of the year with a colony of over 20,000 Orange Diamond-faced Bat, Rhinonicteris aurantia, at Tolmer Falls, in the Northern Territory, but they roost in the areas that are inaccessible to Ghost Bats.

Habitat Ghost Bats occur in a broad range of habitats including arid spinifex hillsides, black soil grasslands, monsoon forest, open savannah woodland, tall open forest, deciduous vine forest and tropical rainforest and agricultural areas. In areas of denser vegetation they follow creek lines and select clearings for hunting. Their distribution is influenced by the availability of suitable caves and mines for roost sites.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  They eat a wide range of dietary items from large invertebrates to birds, bats, small marsupials and rodents, frogs, lizards and geckoes. More than 50 bird species have been recorded as prey, these are almost all small diurnal species (less than 33g) that either aggregate to roost, such as Budgerigars, Melopsittacus undulatus or are ground dwelling, like Little Buttonquail, Turnix velox..They are capable of taking prey up to the size of Grey-crowned Bablers, Pomatostomus temporalis (over 65 g.)

I (S.C.) have watched them repeatedly catch cave bats (mainly Orange Diamond-faced Bats, Rhinonicteris aurantia, on the wing as they leave the roost at dusk, swooping down on them from behind. They are known to feed on over 13 species of bats from small cave dwelling species such as Dusky Leaf-nosed Bats, Hipposideros ater to Common Sheath-tailed Bats, Taphozous georgianus and forest dwelling species such as Gould’s Wattled Bat, Chalinolobus gouldii and White-striped Free-tailed Bats, Austronomus australis. They commonly eat large invertebrates particularly locusts and beetles, but also centipedes, millipedes, spiders, cockroaches, moths, caterpillars, cicadas and even small prey such as ants and termites.
When feeding on small ground dwelling mammals and birds they drop onto them from above and wrap them in their wings, biting them repeatedly on the head. They then hold them by the head and crawl backwards to a tree, climbing the tree with their hind feet until they have gained enough height to fly to a perch before consuming it. They eat their prey headfirst often holding it with their thumb claws, discarding beaks, jaws, wings, legs and tails. Their distinctive claw marks can be seen commonly on the smooth white bark of the Poplar Gum, Eucalyptus alba, at Pine Creek where Ghost Bats are common. The same distinctive scratches are found in the caves where they roost. They often return to their cave roost to eat their food, although this may only be with their final prey for the hunt. Smaller prey are more likely to be eaten at the perch. On Groote Eylandt they were captured in pit traps where they had entered and eaten several of the rare Northern Hopping Mice, Notomys aquillo, that were being studied. There is evidence of Ghost Bats preying upon cane toads in Kakadu National Park, bats have been found dead with chewed cane toads in their throats.
Ghost Bats use several techniques for catching prey: active aerial hunting with echolocation; gleaning the ground or other surfaces while in flight using echolocation; perch hunting to ambush passing prey both on the ground and in the air using echolocation and perch hunting by passively detecting prey using eyesight and/or quietly listening for noises, made by prey, without the use of echolocation. Radio-tracking studies have shown they forage about 2 kms (up to 12 kms at Mt Etna) from the roost cave, and use the same foraging areas (about 60 ha) each night. Within this area the bats hunted from tree perches, moving to a new vantage point every 15 minutes. Foraging areas were not exclusive and up to 20 bats had overlapping ranges. This perch hunting technique is an effective way to conserve energy.

Flight When hunting they have a gently curving, sinuous, moderate-speed flight. They weave between the trees in open woodland, often gliding and their wingbeats are very quiet. While foraging they fly at about 2 to 3 m above the ground, over shrubs and tall grass. Flying from perch to perch and staying for 5 to 20 minutes. Their agility is high; they can make coordinated turns of more than 60o and asymmetrical turns using skidding turns, open wingover and tight wingovers. When commuting they usually fly below 10 m. Flight speeds of 25 km/hr have been recorded. Ghost Bats have the highest wing loading of all the surface gleaning species and consequently needs a higher flight speed to generate sufficient lift even for level flight. In open areas of grassland they often become entangled in barbed wire fences. As their flight is usually just above the vegetation and they often fly without echolocating, they are particularly vulnerable to new fences.

 Vocalisations and Echolocation calls s and vocalisations Ghost Bats make a broad range of calls that are within human hearing consisting of  distinctive chitters, chirps and trills, they make these calls when disturbed at the roost, or when they are getting active before they leave the cave at dusk. These calls can also be heard throughout the forest at night. The calls shown here are courtesy of Nicola Hanrahan.
Ghost Bats have among the most sensitive hearing measured among bats. Their large ears amplify sounds up to 30 dB, especially in frequencies of 4 to 10 kHz, which would help them to readily detect sounds created by their prey.
