Least Blossom Bat
Macroglossus minimus
(Geoffroy, 1810)

Other names Macroglossus lagochilus, Lesser Long-tongued Blossom Bat and Northern Blossom Bat

Description This is the smallest species of Fruit Bat in the world. Like a miniature Flying Fox with long pointed muzzle and very long brush-tipped tongue. The fur is a uniform light reddish brown, slightly paler on the belly. There is a claw on the second digit of the wing. They are small with a forearm of 40.0 (38.1 to 43.0) mm and weight of 12.8 (10.5 to 19.5) grams.

Roost Habits They roost alone or in mother infant pairs or small well spaced groups of 2 to 3. They roost with their wings wrapped around them and look like a cluster of dead leaves. the illusion is enhanced by the bats habit of occasionally rocking from side to side as if the wind has disturbed them. They do not move far between roosts but change roosts frequently. They commonly roost on the underside of large leaves (living or dead), under tree branches, in tree hollows, under loose bark, amongst thick foliage, in bamboo thickets, or in palm fronds. They have also been reported to roost in the rolled up leaves of bananas and under the eaves of abandoned huts. At the day roost they can enter shallow torpor. Their body temperature decreasing as they rest. Their temperature also decreases with reduced ambient temperature. When foraging their metabolic rate is twice the resting rate and although they live in the tropics they enter torpor for several hours every day. This drops their energy expenditure to 60 to 80% of a normal resting rate. Least Blossom Bats have a lower metabolic rate than expected for a mammal of their weight. Habitat They have been found from sea level to 2250m with densities decreasing at higher altitudes. They show a preference for any habitat with large flowers for them to feed on. In Australia that is predominantly rainforest, monsoon forest, mangroves, paperbark swamps, bamboo thickets and riverine vegetation. They can also be found in eucalypt woodlands adjacent to these habitats.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Least Blossom Bats use their large eyes and keen sense of smell to find flowers in the forest at night to to feed on nectar and pollen. They collect nectar with long tongues with rows of modified lingual papillae at the tip that act as a nectar-absorbing mop. They feed on heavily flowering Paperbarks Melaleuca  (particularly the night-flowering Melaleuca cajuputi) and Satinash Syzygium trees, Mangroves (particularly Sonneratia), and they are inordinately fond of Banana Musa flowers (a favourite throughout their range). Other food items include flowering Coconut Palms, Freshwater Mangroves Barringtonia and Bottlebrush Callistemon. Feeds actively upon two introduced plants, the Sausage Tree Kigelia pinnata, and the Century Plant Agave americana both of which are pollinated by bats in their countries of origin. These bats are capable of briefly hovering in front of a flower but they usually crash land and hang sprawled across the flower to feed. Their fur becomes covered in pollen in the process. The hairs are modified with rough scales to catch the pollen which is later groomed off and eaten. Pollen is the major source of protein and nectar provides them with energy. They do eat some fruit, such as Figs Ficus spp, Climbing Pandan Freycinetia spp and Timon Timonius species. These bats can swallow quite large seeds in comparison to their body size and are important pollinators and seed dispersers. Fruits appear to contribute more to their diet in New Guinea than they are in Australia. They also eat leaves and insects commonly are found in their faeces but these are not in high enough numbers to make any significant contribution to their diet. They forage from near ground level well into the sub-canopy. Least Blossom Bats will travel considerable distances for food. In the Philippines there is evidence of them flying between islands. They forage throughout the night. In New Guinea adults hold an exclusive home range of about 6 ha (1.9 to 15.1 ha) with core areas around food plants of 1.5 ha whereas sub-adults usually have overlapping ranges. Adults appear to defend their food resources from other bats by chasing the intruder, vocalising loudly and wing clapping.
One hot, humid night near Darwin, while mist netting in monsoon forest, a Least Blossom Bat hovered repeatedly around my head (SC). It then landed on top of my head and licked the salty sweat from my forehead and face, returning several times for more over the next ten minutes.

Notes These bats have a fast metabolism and burn up energy quickly. Captured bats should be released promptly as they rapidly become low on energy and enter torpor. I (S.C.) have found that feeding them a concentrated sugar and water syrup from a teaspoon gives them enough energy to get going again. It also creates an opportunity to watch their long tongue in action. When captured in mist nets Least Blossom Bats usually remain silent and are relatively easily removed from the net. Southern Blossom Bats, Syconycteris australis, are completely different and will scream loudly and plaintively while struggling. Damian Milne tells me that Least Blossom Bats make a distinctive metallic call that can be heard at night in backyard gardens of Darwin. They have a strong eye shine and can easily been seen when spotlighting.
