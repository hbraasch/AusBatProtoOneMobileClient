East-coast Free-tailed Bat
Micronomus norfolkensis
(Gray, 1839)

Other Names Mormopterus (Micronomus) norfolkensis, Mormopterus norfolkensis, Tadarida norfolkensis Tadarida norfolcensis, Nyctinomus norfolkensis, Molossus norfolkensis, Norfolk Island Free-tailed Bat.

Description A small species, the forearm length is 35.7 – 38.6 mm, with a weight of 6.8 to 10.5 g. The fur on the back is dark brown to reddish brown with the bases cream. The fur on the belly is slightly paler, tricoloured, mainly grey-brown with the tips and bases cream. The ears are triangular, not joined, shorter than the head and being separated by a small patch of fur and. The muzzle is small, tapered to the nostrils, thus differing from Ozimops species where the muzzle is swollen. From the lateral view, the head profile is sinuate, with an elevated braincase and is unlike the flattened and straight profile found in most of the Ozimops species. There is no throat pouch, (previous reports are incorrect). Both males and females possess a long finger-like genital projection. The genital projection is a very useful diagnostic key in the field.

Roost Habits They roost in tree hollows, usually in hollow spouts of large mature trees but there are also several records from buildings. Two males were collected from the wall cavity of a house sharing the roost with eastern Horseshoe Bats, Rhinolophus megaphyllus and a Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens sp. Another was roosting in the roof of a hut with an eastern Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens orion, and several Gould's Wattled Bats Chalinolobus gouldii. They have been collected from under the metal cap on the top of telegraph poles and under exfoliating bark on trees.  They will also roost in bat boxes, and a colony in New South Wales has used the same bat boxes for over 5 years. A pregnant female is reported to have roosted in a telegraph pole in the full sun; a site that it shared with 8 others.

A recent study also identified Grey Mangroves, Avicennia marina, being used as roosts where they were found in hollows in live trees, but also in dead stags. They have also been found in Swamp Oak, Casuarina glauca. The study also found that lactating females switched roosts often, but showed high patch fidelity, with all bats roosting within 1 km of where they were captured and relatively close to their previous roosts (less than 150m). Radio tracking found that bats foraged up to 10 km away from their roosts each night. Other studies have identified them travelling shorter distances; up to 2 km in the Hunter Valley NSW, and up to 5 km at Urbenville in NSW.

Habitat Most records are from dry eucalypt forest and woodland on the coastal side of the Great Dividing Range. Usually only solitary bats are captured, but one group was caught flying low over a rocky river in rainforest and wet sclerophyll forest. They show a preference for open spaces in woodland or forest and are more active in the upper slopes of forest areas rather than in riparian zones. They forage more commonly over larger rather than small waterways.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls . The search phase echolocation call is very variable, but usually comprises short duration pulses of 2–4 msec., usually of constant frequency but can be frequency modulated or slightly dome shaped and in the range of 30–35kHz. Many calls have alternating frequency pulses, the lower pulse anywhere between 1 and 4kHz displaced from the upper pulse. Even though the calls are variable, alternating frequency pulse calls are not known from any other Australian molossid species, thus making calls of M. norfolkensis readily identifiable.
