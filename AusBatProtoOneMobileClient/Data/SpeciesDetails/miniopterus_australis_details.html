Little Long-fingered Bat
Miniopterus australis
(Tomes, 1858)

Other Names Little Bent-wing Bat, Little Bent-winged Bat, Little Bentwing Bat

Description: The little Long-fingered bat has a uniform dark chocolate brown fur on back, slightly lighter on belly. They occasionally have reddish fur due to ammonia bleaching in large nursery caves. Distinctly short muzzle and domed head. The ears are short, rounded and roughly triangular. The last phalanx on the third finger of the wing is about three times the length of the middle phalanx, giving a bent wing appearance. The last phalanx is folded back along the first phalanx when the bat is at rest. This is the smallest Long-fingered bat, with a forearm of 37.3 to 40.8 mm.

Roost Habits These are cave-dwelling bats that congregate in the summer months into maternity colonies and disperse during the winter. The few documented maternity sites are normally situated in limestone cave systems. There is a huge maternity colony of 100,000 adult bats at Mt Etna caves, in central Queensland. They are known to roost in caves, abandoned mines, tunnels, stormwater drains, occasionally buildings and they have even been reported to roost in banana trees within a plantation. In November a colony of 30 bats was found roosting in a crevice in a hollow of a large rainforest tree Citronella moorei in northern New South Wales. Conditions inside the tree hollow were 21oC and 81% humidity (cooler and more humid than outside). It appeared to be used only as an occasional roost and the 3 bats captured were males. They have been found from sea level to about 1,500 m elevation.

Habitat Little Long-fingered bats show a preference for well timbered areas including rainforest, vine thicket, wet and dry sclerophyll forests, Melaleuca swamps and coastal forests.

Diet, Flight and Foraging This species is an aerial hawker, foraging mainly in open spaces. These bats have a relatively low wing loading allowing them to fly rapidly and with considerable maneuverability between shrub and canopy layers of densely wooded areas. Flight speeds of up to 25 km per hour have been recorded. In the Richmond Range in New South Wales their diet contained mostly beetles, moths and flies but also a surprisingly high proportion of spiders (30% of scats contained spiders). Elsewhere they additionally have been reported to eat crane flies, ants and wasps.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls

Characteristic frequency 54.5 to 64.5 kHz (n = 53). This species is known to exhibit some variation in call characteristics over its range, calling at lower frequencies (56 to 56.5 kHz) in north Queensland (Reinhold et al. 2001).
The call is curved, usually with down-sweeping tail and has a higher characteristic frequency than any other species with curved pulses.
