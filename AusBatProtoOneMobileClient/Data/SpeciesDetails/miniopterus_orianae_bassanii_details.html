South Australian Long-fingered Bat
Miniopterus orianae bassanii
(Cardinal and Christidis, 2000)

Other Names Miniopterus schreibersii, Southern Bent-winged Bat, Common Bent-winged Bat (part), Common Bentwing Bat (part). We have adopted the name ‘long-fingered’ as a preferred common name to be consistent with the common names of other overseas species.

There are three subspecies of the Australian Long-fingered Bat, Miniopterus orianae. Further taxonomic assessment is underway, and it is likely that the three sub-species will be eventually upgraded to full species. In the meantime we are using a separate account for each sub-species to avoid confusing what we know about these bats.

Description This bat has dark reddish brown to dark brown fur on its back, slightly lighter on belly. It has a distinctly short muzzle and domed head. The ears are short, rounded and roughly triangular. The last phalanx on the third finger of the wing is about three times the length of the middle phalanx, giving a bent wing appearance. The last phalanx is folded back along the first phalanx when the bat is at rest. The three sub-species of M. orianae are very similar but differ genetically and form separate maternity colonies. The Southern Long-fingered cannot be identified from the East Australian Long-fingered Bats. DNA analysis is required for identification in the species overlap zone in western Victoria.

Roost Habits This subspecies is an obligate cave dweller but also use man made constructions such as mine adits and road culverts. They form discrete populations centred on a maternity cave that is used annually for the birth and development of young. Only two maternity caves are known for the southern Long-fingered bat. Both males and females congregate in many thousands in a dome shaped chamber of the cave. The large numbers of bats roosting together generates a lot of body heat which is trapped in the dome roof. It is in these pockets of warm and humid air that the young are born and reared. The bats’ combined body heat can raise the cave temperature by up to 12oC. Each population disperses to other caves from April to August but only within its specific territorial range. Movement between territories is unusual. Over 50 caves are known to be used as wintering sites. Winter caves need to be cold enough to keep their body temperature low, to reduce their metabolic rate and prolong the fat reserves built up during the summer.

Habitat They are associated primarily with woodlands near large natural wetlands, river basins and agricultural areas.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Moths make up the major part of the diet. Where there are trees this species flies high, from just above the canopy to many times canopy height. In more open areas such as grasslands, flight may be within 6 m of the ground. Flight is very fast and relatively level with swift, shallow dives. They can forage long distances from the roost site.

Activity and Movements
Extensive banding studies were undertaken by Peter Dywer in the 1960s, and there are several records of individuals moving between the Warrnambool/Naracoorte population and sites north-east of Melbourne and in south-east NSW within the range of the Eastern Bent-wing Bat. The longest movement recorded was of an adult male banded in 1963 in north-east NSW and recaptured in 1965 at a cave at Panmure, near Warrnambool, a distance of 1300 km. As there are very few of these long distance records compared to the number of recaptures within the normal range, it is assumed that these are not typical but represent vagrant or possibly human assisted movements. The development of field techniques to distinguish between the Southern and Eastern subspecies would assist in determining the distribution of the Southern Bent-wing Bat. Non-genetic techniques could include morphometric or echolocation call differences, as the two subspecies have been shown to call at different frequencies.

Echolocation Vocalisations and Echolocation calls s for the three sub-species of M. orianae differ from each other. The southern Long-fingered bat M. o. bassanii has a mean frequency of 47.7 kHz.
