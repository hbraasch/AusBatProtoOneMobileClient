East Australian Long-fingered Bat
Miniopterus orianae oceanensis
Maeda, 1982

Other Names Miniopterus schreibersii, Eastern Bent-winged Bat, Common Bent-winged Bat (part), Common Bentwing Bat (part).

Description This bat has dark reddish brown to dark brown fur on its back, slightly lighter on belly. A reddish colour form is known from Queensland. It has a distinctly short muzzle and domed head. The ears are short, rounded and roughly triangular. The last phalanx on the third finger of the wing is about three times the length of the middle phalanx, giving a bent wing appearance. The last phalanx is folded back along the first phalanx when the bat is at rest.

Roost Habits East Australian Long-fingered Bats are cave dwellers but also use man made structures such as abandoned mines and road culverts. Populations are centred on maternity caves that are used annually for the birth and development of young. Each population disperses to other caves during the year but only within its own territorial range. Movement between territories is unusual, although one bat is recorded to have moved 1,300 kms. In the southern Australian winter these bats select hibernation caves that are cold enough to keep their body temperature low, to reduce their metabolic rate and prolong fat reserves built up during the summer. In the tropical "winter" they remain active and forage nightly, selecting roost caves of moderate temperatures.

Habitat They occur is a broad range of habitats including rainforest, wet and dry sclerophyll forest, monsoon forest, open woodland, paperbark forests and open grasslands. 

Diet, Flight and Foraging  At Richmond Range in New South Wales moths were the dominant prey item with a few flies, cockroaches and beetles. In forested areas this species flies high, from just above the canopy to many times canopy height. In more open areas such as grasslands, flight may be within 6m of the ground. Flight is very fast and typically relatively level with swift shallow dives. They can forage long distances from the roost site and several marked females have travelled up to 65 km in one night.

Vocalisations and Echolocation Calls

The characteristic frequency is 43 to 48 kHz (n = 72). Curved, often with down-sweeping tail. Characteristic section may be long in search phase. Pulse shape and time between calls usually variable within a sequence.
