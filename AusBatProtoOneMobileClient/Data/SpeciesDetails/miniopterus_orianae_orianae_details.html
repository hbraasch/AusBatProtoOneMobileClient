North Australian Long-fingered Bat
Miniopterus orianae orianae
Thomas, 1922

Other Names Miniopterus schreibersii, Southern Bent-winged Bat, Common Bent-winged Bat (part), Common Bentwing Bat (part).

Description This bat has dark reddish brown to dark brown fur on its back, slightly lighter on belly. It has a distinctly short muzzle and domed head. The ears are short, rounded and roughly triangular. The last phalanx on the third finger of the wing is about three times the length of the middle phalanx, giving a bent wing appearance. The last phalanx is folded back along the first phalanx when the bat is at rest.

Roost Habits North Australian Long-fingered Bats roost in a variety of caves and mines from shallow sandstone overhangs to complex limestone labyrinths, abandoned mines, road culverts, storm water drains and bridges as well as buildings. From April to August in the tropical "winter" bats do not hibernate, instead they remain active and forage nightly, selecting roost caves of moderate temperatures (26.5oC) and high humidity (95%). This choice of roost microclimate enables them to maintain water balance at minimum metabolic cost. They usually roost clinging to the walls in large clusters in close contact with each other. 

Habitat They use a broad range of habitats including rainforest, wet and dry sclerophyll forest, monsoon forest, open woodland, paperbark forests and open grasslands. They are usually associated with rocky hills and escarpments.

Diet, Flight and Foraging Moths are the major item of diet. In the Kimberley their stomachs also contained grasshoppers and in the Top End they also ate leaf-hoppers, ants, occasional beetles, flies and spiders. In forested areas this species flies high, from just above the canopy to many times canopy height. In more open areas such as grasslands and over water, they fly lower, within a couple of metres of the ground. Flight is very fast and typically relatively level with swift shallow dives. Flight speeds of 20 to 32 km/hr have been recorded. When exiting from a cave their flight is sharply zigzagged presumably to reduce predation. They are often predated upon by Ghost Bats Macroderma gigas as they exit the cave.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls 
Characteristic frequency 48.5 kHz, similar in many respects to Pipistrellus westralis. The pulse shape is curvilinear and has a relatively high initial section and usually no or at most a very short tail. Although the frequency range for M. o. orianae extends slightly higher than Pipistrellus westralis, it is considered that it can not be confidently separated given the limited number of reference calls collected for P.westralis.
