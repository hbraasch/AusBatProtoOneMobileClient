Flute-nosed Bat
Murina florium
Thomas, 1908

Other Names Insectivorous Tube-nosed Bat,

Description This elusive and unusual bat has tubular nostrils that diverge and extend sideways from the end of the nose. The fur on the back is grey-brown to an orange rufous-brown with the base of the fur dark to light grey. The fur is long and woolly with a grizzled appearance and is paler on the belly. The fur extends over most of the tail membrane and along the bones of the wings and legs. The ears are short and rounded with the rear margins deeply notched. The wing membrane is attached along the outer edge of the first toe. The thumbs are unusually long. They emit a loud drawn-out, high-pitched whistle while foraging.

Habitat Captures have been in both upland and lowland tropical rainforest (microphyll fern-vine, notophyll vine & mesophyll forest) often with emergent eucalypts. They are also found in tall open forest of flooded gums (Eucalyptus grandis) with or without rainforest understorey, in gallery forests, occasionally in dry sclerophyll forest and disturbed forest. They are found from the lowlands to elevations of 1,200 m (up to 2,800 m outside Australia). They are more common at higher elevations. 

Roost Habits These distinctive bats live in the wet rainforests of north Queensland. They roost in the understorey 1 to 8 m from the ground wherever they find suitable cover. They are known to roost in suspended clusters of fallen leaves, the curled dead base of epiphytes, tree fern and palm fronds, disused nests of Fernwrens (Oreoscopus gutturalis) and Yellow-throated Scrubwrens (Sericornis citreogularis). The bird nests often have a hole in the base that may have been made by the bat. All roosts have been in simple microphyll vine forest. Over 80% of roosts are occupied by a single bat but up to a dozen may share a simple roost such as a curled dead leaf. One individual was found roosting in the nest of a Sacred Kingfisher (Todiramphus sanctus) inside a termite mound in a tree. Several bats released during the day landed on broad leaves and wrapped them around, holding the leaves in this position with their feet and thumb claws so that they could not be seen except from directly below. This bat is also unusual in the way it roosts hanging by its toes and wrapping its wings around its body, like a flying fox but keeping the wings out from the chest to form an “umbrella”. 

Diet, Flight and Foraging They forage in the canopy and sub-canopy and avoid open areas. Their wings are long and have low wing loading and low aspect ratio suggesting agile and manoeuvrable flight at low speed. Individuals emerging at dusk hover briefly under roosts and exit the roost area through vine tendrils, branches and other cluttered situations. They have a very slow and manoeuvrable flight. Scat analysis has shown their diet to consist of two-thirds beetles, one-third spiders, with traces of moths and flies. Coprophagy (eating its own faces) has been observed in this bat in captivity. It is the only Australian bat known to do this. In captivity they have been observed to roost to eat their prey. They Initially land with their feet and then hook onto the branch with their long thumbs and release their feet. Keeping the body curled around they hang by their thumbs bringing the tail up close to the face to catch any food that escapes their mouth. This method was observed and filmed several times by Jenny McLean at the Tolga Bat Hospital with a captive Murina eating mealworms.

Vocalisation and Echolocation Flute-nosed Bats emit low-intensity wide frequency range echolocation calls of short duration (1.5 to 2 ms). The combination of manoeuvrable flight characteristics and broad frequency range echolocation calls indicates this bat is specialised for foraging within a cluttered environment. The high frequency calls are thought to allow sophisticated texture resolution enabling the spectral discrimination of stationary prey.  They are very vocal while foraging producing a drawn-out high pitched audible whistle.  This social call occurs in 20 millisecond bursts at 10 kHz. It is the only Australian bat to make this type of call (giving it the name Flute-nosed Bat). 

Notes Flute-nosed Bats have extremely large olfactory areas of the brain and the large tube-like nostrils indicate that their sense of smell is very well developed, an unusual characteristic for echolocating bats.
