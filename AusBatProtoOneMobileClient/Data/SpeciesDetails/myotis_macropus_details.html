Large-footed Myotis
Myotis macropus
(Gould, 1855)

Other Names Myotis adversus

Description This fascinating fishing bat can be distinguished from all other Australian vespertilionid bats by its disproportionately large feet, (8 to 11 mm, not including claws), they are almost half the length of the tibia, if not larger. Its fur colour is variable from grey to dark brown above, slightly paler below; old bats may have ginger coloured fur. Albinisim (or partial albinism) is not uncommon. The ears are long with a rounded tip and the tragus is long, straight and slender. The calcar (a cartilage strut extending from the ankle and supporting the rear edge of the tail membrane) is very long, extending more than half way from the ankle to the tail tip.

Habitat They are always near water and have a strong association with streams and permanent waterways, most frequently at low elevations and in flat or undulating country and usually in areas that are vegetated rather than cleared. They will live in most habitat types ranging from rainforest streams to inland rivers, large lakes and reservoirs. This also includes brackish and salt water habitats such as creeks and inlets amongst mangroves.

Roost Habits Large-footed Myotis roost near water in caves, tree hollows, amongst vegetation, in clumps of Pandanus, under bridges, in mines, tunnels, road culverts and storm water drains. They are commonly found roosting alone or in pairs in abandoned intact fairy martin nests. They will select caves that overhang pools even when these are rather exposed. Colonies of several hundred are known but more commonly they roost in groups of less than 15. In the breeding season  each male establishes a territory, excludes other males and forms a harem of 1 to 12 females. When not breeding, males either roost alone or form bachelor groups of up to 20 individuals. Older males can be recognised by the scars on their ears resulting from aggressive encounters when defending their territory.

Diet, Flight and Foraging The Large-footed Myotis, Myotis macropus, is the only bat in Australia specialised for foraging directly over water surfaces. Such highly specialised feeding ecology restricts the distribution of M. macropus to coastal regions and inland waterways. They commonly forage over water for small fish, prawns and aquatic insects such as water boatmen, water striders, backswimmers and whirligig beetles. These are mainly species that live on or just below the water surface and are caught by trawling.

The bats may forage individually but more commonly they hunt together. Sometimes several bats follow each other while foraging along similar flight paths.

The bats fly 15 to 100 cm above the water frequently dipping their big feet into the water and briefly raking them along the surface.  They have a preference for large still pools rather than flowing streams. It is thought that they use echolocation to detect the small ripples made by prey on the water surface. These bats are also aerial foragers, usually hunting insects that fly over water. In the Top End their diet is more terrestrial with scats containing mostly termites, flies, ants, moths, beetles, spiders, cockroaches and bugs. They also forage over salt water in mangroves and coastal areas with calm conditions.

Flight observations made in the Kimberley show that when foraging over water,  Large Footed Myotis have relatively fast flight, low over the water surface (1 cm to 1 m high), with frequent changes in direction, but not extreme turns. All turns were horizontal banks (banked up to 70o), they could make these agile turns at full speed of 26 to 30 kph. When foraging on land they use moderate to slow flight, low to the ground, less than 1 to 2 m occasionally up to 4 m above the grass. They use straight or curving sinuous flight with long circling, co-ordinated turns with no abrupt manoeuvres. They often fly a repeated pattern over the water, foraging back and forth along the edge of the vegetation and zigzagging out over open water.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls Steep, near vertical calls, starting at between 70 to 80 kHz usually dropping to between 35 to 40 kHz (n = 21). Good quality calls have a central kink around 47 to 50kHz and very occasionally another prior to the tail dropping off around 35 kHz. Appears very similar to calls of Nyctophilus and could be easily confused.

Conservation M. macropus is a habitat specialist, reliant on intact, mature riparian vegetation and relatively open, permanent water ways. Such ecological specialisation has led to the species’ restricted distribution and apparently low population densities. Significant population structuring in Victoria implies movement of individuals along waterways and between catchments is infrequent and that migratory behaviour during winter is unlikely. Given the inherent increased extinction risk faced by isolated, small populations of specialist species, management actions should be directed towards maintenance, enhancement, and linkage of riparian vegetation, including the retention of older habitat trees that are more likely to contain hollows. These actions should promote the movement of this uncommon species throughout the landscape.
