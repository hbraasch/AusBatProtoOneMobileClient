Queensland Tube-nosed Bat
Nyctimene robinsoni
Thomas, 1904

Other Names Eastern Tube-nosed Bat, Queensland Tube-nosed Fruit Bat

Description A beautifully marked fruit bat distinguished from other species by its long, protruding, tube-like nostrils and bright yellow or green spots splashed across its wings and ears. The fur is grey to reddish brown all over with a distinct narrow dark black stripe down along the spine.
The tubular divergent nostrils are capable of opening and closing and moving independently of one another. They are thought  that the nostrils work in stereo to help locate fruit at night by their odour plumes. The eyes are large with a amber coloured irises and they give a strong eye shine at night. The upper incisor teeth are present but it does not have lower incisors (they are lost as juveniles) and the modified lower canines, are almost touching. The tail is quite long but is mostly free of the tail membrane, except where it joins at the base. The flight membrane between the hind legs is reduced to just a narrow strip, about 10 mm wide. The wings join the feet at the second toe.
They make a distinctive whistling call while in flight. In north Queensland they can be heard in the forest most nights, often several bats calling at once. In New South Wales they only call in the warmer months from October to April and are usually solitary.

Roost Habits Queensland Tube-nosed Bats roost during the day in foliage in the rainforest sub-canopy layer where they find effective camouflage amongst the dead leaves. Individuals generally roost alone approximately 4 to 6 m above the ground to higher up and out of sight in the canopy. They roost with their wings wrapped around them and look like a cluster of dead leaves. The illusion is enhanced by the yellow spots that provide a sun dappled appearance and bats’ habit of occasionally rocking from side to side as if the wind has disturbed them. Even very young bats learn this rocking habit even while hanging on to Mum. They are very inconspicuous, even on quite exposed foliage. One evening after measuring two individuals I had netted, I placed them side by side on a low, nearby branch, about 2 m above the ground. Expecting them to fly away during the night I gave them no further thought. The following afternoon when packing up camp I noticed they were still there although I must have walked within half a metre of them repeatedly for most of the previous night and all that day. Both animals watched me through slitted eyes, whenever I approached, but made no other movement. Some individuals roost at the same place each day for years, others roost in the last tree they were feeding in before dawn. They usually roost alone but groups of up to five animals have been found roosting together.

Habitat They occur in tropical rainforest, preferring mature primary forest rather than secondary regrowth forest; sub-tropical rainforest, complex notophyll vine forest, Araucarian notophyll vine forests, gallery forests and wet sclerophyll forests. In subtropical areas they favour stream-side habitats within coastal rainforest and moist eucalypt forests with a well developed rainforest understorey. In New Guinea they occur in lowland rainforest and monsoon forest with open understorey and virtually no shrub and ground cover.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They eat a variety of fleshy fruit from both understorey rainforest trees and canopy trees, favouring figs (Ficus spp.such as F. varigata, F. congesta, F. copiosa, F. nodosa and F. watkinsiana), native cherries Eugenia spp, lillipillies Syzigium spp and False Gardenia Atractocarpus sessilis, and many cauliferous fruits, which they consume either on the spot or carry away. They are also extremely partial to many introduced fruits such as Guava Psidium guajava and Soursop Annona muricata.

In New South Wales they tend to forage in the canopy over 20m above the ground on rainforest tree fruits, including Maiden‘s Blush Sloanea australis, Yellow Carabeen S. woolsii, Morton Bay Chestnut Castanospermum australe, Bangalow Palm Archontophoenix cunninghamiana, and Mistletoes. They also feed on the blossoms of Burdekin Plum Pleigynium timorense, Blue Quondong Eleocarpus grandis and Eucalyptus species.

They mainly eat firm to hard fruit that they hold against their chest with their thumb claws and take large neat bites with their modified lower canines. They chew the food for a considerable time and then push it hard against the ridges on the roof of the mouth with their tongue and squeeze all the liquid from it before spitting out the dry spat. The tongue has 4 circumvallate papillae (two large and two small) and has a complex papillae arrangement and structure of an obligate frugivore. A small scattering of dry spat can sometimes be found beneath favoured fruit trees (such as guavas).

They have large broad wings for their size enabling them to carry heavy loads of fruit (up to 30 g or half their own body weight) and to hover, turn around in mid-air, and manoeuvre in dense vegetation. Queensland Tube-nosed Bats have a pronounced daily activity pattern with their body temperature being lowest just after sunset and the highest just before dawn. Their average daily core temperature ranges from 34 to 38oC to as low as 32.6oC.

They remain active throughout the night until just before dawn except on strongly moonlit nights where they remain at the roost until the moon sets to avoid predation. During the tropical winter the bats travel only very short distances (100 to 420 m) between feeding sites and different roost locations, and distances between consecutive roost sites averaged 40m, thereby using comparatively little energy for traveling, and saving energy for other activities. The energy expenditure for this bat living in areas of moderate temperature fluctuations and high food abundance is relatively low and does not require the use of torpor for energy conservation. However the bats may use torpor during adverse weather or at the subtropical southern end of their distribution range. The basal metabolic rate for Queensland Tube-nosed Bats was measured at 54.7mlO2h-1 which is in the range reported for other similar sized fruit bats

 Notes Queensland tube-nosed fruit bats are important seed dispersers and pollinators for Australian rainforest trees. They are the only small, understory, seed-dispersing bat. They carry seeds in their gut but also carry fruit away to a feeding roost that may be a considerable distance from the fruiting tree.
