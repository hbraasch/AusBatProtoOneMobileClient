Arnhem Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus arnhemensis
(Johnson, 1959)

Other NamesNorthern Long-eared Bat

Description The Arnhem Long-eared Bat is a medium sized species (forearm 33.2 to 40 mm) with moderately long ears (16 to 21.5 mm). The fur is usually light russet brown with dark bases to the hairs. The fur shades to a slightly lighter colour on the belly but is not distinctly different. The muzzle ridge is moderately developed and has a central depression. The glans penis is a square ended cylinder with a flat circular urethral opening on the underside, near the tip. The Pallid Long-eared Bat, N. daedalus is significantly larger, with larger forearm 38.3 to 45.8 mm), longer ears (20.5 to 25.8 mm), less developed muzzle ridge, a similar fur colour, and the glans penis is divided by a longitudinal groove into two cylinders with the upper cylinder projecting to give a distinct beaked appearance, OCW 5.1 mm (4.7 to 5.5 mm). The Pygmy Long-eared Bat, N. walkeri, is much smaller in all measurements, it has much shorter ears (10.7 to 14.1 mm) and smaller forearm (30.1 to 36.0 mm), narrower muzzle OCW of 3.6 mm (3.4 to 3.7 mm) with the belly fur distinctly paler than back. The lesser Long-eared Bat, N. geoffroyi has a well developed muzzle ridge and white belly fur.

Roost Habits These bats have been found roosting 2 to 3 m above the ground under hanging flaps of the soft, flexible bark of large paperbark trees (Melaleuca leucodendron). They will also shelter in houses, within rainforest foliage and amongst Pandanus leaves.

Habitat They have been caught in large numbers in and around mangroves as well as monsoon forest, open savannah woodland, tall open forest, and Melaleuca, Pandanus lined waterways and over waterholes in sandstone country. In the Kimberley they were found foraging in rainforest patches, in groves of cajuput and Melaleuca argentia forest that fringe watercourses, and in woodlands of Tristania grandiflora and Melaleuca acacioides around freshwater swamps. Their distribution tends to correspond to the areas that receive an annual rainfall of over 500 mm.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They hunt amongst and alongside the foliage of dense vegetation. Prey is gleaned from surfaces or caught in flight at relatively slow speeds of 5 km/hr but they can fly at up to 24km/hr when commuting. While foraging, the slow fluttering flight afforded by their short, broad wings is well adapted to confined spaces where manoeuvrability is preferable to speed. They are often seen flying through the vegetation or very low along tracks and waterways, less than 50cm above the surface. Their flight is very slow and irregular with frequent changes of direction and height, making gentle coordinated turns often in tight curves. They are capable of hovering and in the Top End their diet has been found to be composed of predominantly termites, water-beetles, cockroaches, field crickets, bugs and occasional moths and spiders. They have been also been recorded feeding on geckos and gecko eggs but the details of this observation are unknown. Its search-mode echolocation pulses are broadly tuned with most energy between 49–53 kHz and, to increase their effective range, call sequences include occasional sub-sequences that step down to 38 kHz. Northern Long-eared Bats commute at steady, level flight speeds of about 24 kph.
Reproduction  Males have scrotal testes from May to November. Females are pregnant in September and November, twins are born from late October to February. Young are born, usually twins, from late in the dry season (October) to the end of the wet season (February).

Vocalisations and Vocalisations and Echolocation calls NT Characteristic frequency 49 to 53kHz. Calls are similar to Myotis macropus, as well as other Nyctophilus species and are linear with the length of each call varying considerably within a sequence.
