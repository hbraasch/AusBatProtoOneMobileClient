Eastern Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus bifax
(Thomas, 1915)

 Description A large species of Nyctophilus, with a forearm of 37.5 to 46.8 mm. The fur colour is usually a rich brown-tan, often with the belly fur a lighter colour. The ears are long (19.2 to 27.1 mm). The muzzle ridge is poorly developed with a slight central depression (Type 3). The glans penis is a square ended cylinder with a flat circular urethral opening on the underside, near the tip. The baculum is forked at the tip. Gould's Long-eared Bat, N. gouldi, differ by usually having longer ears (greater than 24 mm), a better developed muzzle ridge (Type 2) and males have a distinctly beaked glans penis. Corben’s Long-eared Bat, Nyctophilus corbeni is found further inland, to the west of the Great Dividing Range. It is larger with a broader muzzle and an OCW greater than 5.7 whereas N. bifax is less than 5.3 mm. The Lesser Long-eared Bat N. geoffroyi is smaller with a better developed muzzle ridge (Type 1).

Roost Habits Eastern Long-eared Bats are forest dwellers and have been recorded roosting under peeling bark, among epiphytes, in tree hollows and shallow depressions on trunks and branches, in the roots of strangler figs, amongst the dead fronds of a prickly tree fern and in foliage. Tree hollows are used more than foliage when the females are lactating but foliage roosts are more commonly used during the mating season. In northern New South Wales, eastern Long-eared Bats shift foliage roosts seasonally to the cooler forest interior in November (late spring) and back to the warmer forest edge in May (late autumn). Roost mobility over seasons may be necessary for foliage–roosting bats as such roosts are not thermally buffered. They have also been reported to roost in buildings. Colonies of 2 to 7 have been recorded (those examined contained only females). These aggregations were loose, and females moved between colonies. Males tended to roost alone. Bats changed roost frequently but showed a strong fidelity to one cluster of trees. Most roosts were less than 250 m apart. At the southern end of their range, Eastern Long-eared Bats, Nyctophilus bifax, have been found to lower their body temperature and enter torpor every day throughout winter. Both males and females exhibited multi-day torpor bouts of up to 5.4 days. Torpor bouts were longer during periods of lower temperatures. During summer when insects were abundant male bats used torpor on 85% of days and 38% of nights. Torpor bouts last for 1 to 21 hours. Individuals with large fat reserves had longer and deeper torpor bouts and entered torpor more often than those with low body fat reserves. Although torpor is effective for energy conservation it also entails costs, such as reduced foetal development in pregnant females. Even in the subtropics torpor is used regularly by non reproductive males to conserve energy but is rarely used by pregnant females as it slowed foetal development.

Habitat They favour wetter habitats ranging from rainforest and monsoon forest to riverine forests of paperbark but are also found in on open woodland, tall open forest and dry sclerophyll woodland. In northern NSW they are restricted to rainforest.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. At Chillagoe, Queensland they have been observed perch hunting. The bats typically hang from perches 5 to 10 m above the ground and pivot their body back and forth while echolocating. They periodically make short, less than 3 m, flights from the perch presumably to catch prey. After 3 to 5 minutes with no foraging flights the bats moved to another perch, usually within 50 m. Dietary analysis has recorded moths, with traces of ants and click-beetles in the stomach and scats. They tend to forage along the edge of the tree canopy rather than within the foliage. As well as perch hunting they catch prey by aerial capture and gleaning off surfaces. In rainforest they were found to forage in the canopy over 25 m above the ground.

Vocalisations and Vocalisations and Echolocation calls. Steep, near vertical, starting at between 70 to 108 kHz usually dropping to between 47 to 55 kHz (n = 10). Good quality calls usually have two changes in the slope in the middle or lower half. The first section is longest and steepest followed by a flatter section and then a steeper tail. All Nyctophilus spp. are soft callers so short fragmentary calls are typical. Call characteristics and frequencies almost completely overlap with Nyctophilus gouldi, N. geoffroyii, and N. corbeni making them indistinguishable using standard Anabat / Analook parameters.
