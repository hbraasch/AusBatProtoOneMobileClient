Corben’s Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus corbeni
Parnaby, 2009

 

Other Names Nyctophilus timoriensis, Nyctophilus major (part), Greater Long-eared bat, South-eastern Long-eared Bat.

Description A rare species that occurs patchily across suitable habitat, this species has only recently been formally described. The fur is dark sandy brown to grey brown, and only slightly lighter brown on the tips. The fur on the belly may be pale. The muzzle ridge is low and lacks a vertical groove. Comparing the sizes of the larger Long-eared Bat species can be confusing, and so we suggest that attention should be first directed to the outer canine width (OCW) for these species. N. corbeni has an OCW that is greater than 5.6 mm; N. gouldi and N. bifax have OCW’s in the range of 4.8 to 5.4mm but have distinctly different muzzle ridges (which need to be examined with a hand lens). Please refer to the muzzle images herein.  

Roost Habits In South Australia they roost in hollows of live trees including Casuarina pauper, Myoporum platycarpum and the mallee Eucalyptus gracilis. In Queensland they roost in bulloak, brigalow/belah communities and ironbark woodlands. Roost hollows are reported to be less than 3m above the ground; with multiple small entrances of 5 to 10cm. Elsewhere they roost in fissures in branches and under dried sheets of bark still attached to the trunks of ring-barked trees. They forage up to 3 km from the roost. Tree hollows are also used as maternity sites.

Habitat Corben’s Long-eared Batsare most abundant where the vegetation has a distinct canopy and a dense, cluttered understorey layer. They inhabit a wide variety of vegetation types including River Red Gum, Eucalyptus camaldulensis, Black Box, Allocasuarina, Belah, Mallee, open woodlands and savannahs. They are most common in box, ironbark and cypress open forests and bulloke woodlands of inland northern New South Wales. In South Australia they are confined to tall mallee shrublands. In Queensland they have been recorded from semi-evergreen vine thicket with Brachychiton emergents, inland dry sclerophyll forest with Corymbia citriodora and ironbark, open forest with grass trees, Callitris forest, mixed eucalypt forest, poplar box open forest, open forest with mid-storey of Allocasuarina and Callitris. It is very common in the Pilliga Scrub in New South Wales where it comprised up to 9 percent of all bat captures.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They are highly manoeuvrable bats that are able to change direction often and quickly. They fly very close to vegetation, often weaving through the canopy but also flying through gaps. They have been seen to catch insects by first flying high and then swooping almost to the ground. They usually fly at canopy height (4 to 5 m) but this depends on the vegetation type through which they are foraging. They hunt by aerial foraging and by gleaning their prey from the surface of vegetation or the ground. Their diet has not been examined.

Vocalisations and Vocalisations and Echolocation calls Calls are almost identical in shape and characteristics to other Nyctophilus species and are near vertical, starting at between 60 and 80 kHz, usually dropping to between 31 to 37 kHz (n = 16).
