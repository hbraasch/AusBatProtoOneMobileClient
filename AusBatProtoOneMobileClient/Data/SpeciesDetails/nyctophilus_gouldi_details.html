Gould's Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus gouldi
Tomes, 1858

Other Names Nyctophilus timoriensis gouldi, Greater Long-eared Bat

Description This well-studied species has slate grey to grey-brown fur on the back with an ash-grey belly. The size varies from north to south being much larger in the south. The ears are long (24.3 to 30.1 mm). The muzzle ridge is moderately developed with a faint vertical groove. The glans penis is divided by a longitudinal groove into two cylinders, with the upper one projecting to give a distinctly beak-like appearance at the tip. The eastern Long-eared Bat, N. bifax, differs in having a less developed muzzle ridge, usually shorter ears (19.2 to 27.1 mm) and the glans penis is a square ended cylinder that is not beaked. N gouldi has an outer canine width (OCW) of less than 5.1 mm, which separates this species from Corben’s Long-eared Bat Nyctophilus corbeni with OCW’s of over 5.6 mm.

Roost Habits These bats roost in tree hollows, in fissures and under exfoliating bark, even in hollows in fallen logs. Often, a particular group of trees, often along a creek line, is used for roosting. Within this group they select new roosts frequently, usually moving about 160m daily, sometimes returning after 2 to 4 days. Females form colonies of 20 or more, while males roost alone or in small transient groups of less than 6. Lactating females form maternity colonies in tree hollows often in dead trees. They move their young to new roosts a distance of 100 to 200m, every night.

In southern Australia they lay down body fat in late summer and hibernate from April until September.

Habitat They are found in a variety of habitats from very wet to semi-arid environments. They occur in rainforest, wet and dry sclerophyll forest, Melaleuca woodland, river red gum lined waterways, woodlands and Acacia shrubland.

Diet, Flight and Foraging These bats typically fly slowly in large circles approximately 2 to 5 m above the ground, and occasionally within the canopy of forest trees. They have been observed to land on the ground to catch prey. Their diet consists of mainly moths and beetles, but crickets, flies, cockroaches, ants, bugs and spiders are also taken. Insects are caught on the wing or gleaned from surfaces of vegetation or the ground using a variety of techniques, such as passive listening, vision and echolocation. Although their echolocation is highly sophisticated it is not used for orientation except in unfamiliar environments, nor is it used when they approach fluttering prey, as they rely instead on passive listening. This behaviour circumvents the evasive action of sonar-sensitive insects. They can also perch-hunt using passive listening. Visual cues are used to capture flying insects but not substrate prey.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls are almost identical in shape and characteristics to other Nyctophilus species. They are near vertical, starting at between 65 and 80 kHz, usually dropping to between 35 to 43 kHz (n = 56).
