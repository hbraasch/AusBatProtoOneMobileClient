Holt’s Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus holtorum
Parnaby et al., 2021

Description A recently described species from Western Australia, previously included with Nyctophilus gouldi which is now recognised as occurring only in eastern Australia. All publications relating to N. gouldi from WA are most likely referable to this new species.

Like its eastern Australian counterpart, this species has slate grey to grey-brown fur on the back with an ash-grey belly. The muzzle ridge is moderately developed with a faint by nevertheless distinct, vertical groove. Forearm is 38.5 – 43.7 mm and OCW is 4.3 - 4.8 mm. The latter can be used to separate this species from N. major and N. major tor, both of which have larger OCW values.

Roost Habits These bats are likely to roost in tree hollows, in fissures and under exfoliating bark. Colonies of up to 25 have been found roosting in the dead leaves that skirt the base of grass trees (Xanthorea sp.).  

Habitat This species is found primarily in eucalypt forest and woodlands in wet temperate environments. It is likely that understorey components are important in these habitats.  

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Like other long-eared bats, this species appears to be well adapted to forage in moderate to dense clutter, in and around vegetation. They may also glean insects from leaf surfaces and land on the ground to catch prey. The may also perch hunt in the understorey.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls Almost vertical pulses that appear very similar to other Nyctophilus and may be very difficult to separate from other sympatric species.
