Western Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus major major
Gray, 1844

Other Names Nyctophilus timoriensis gouldi, Greater Long-eared Bat

Description In general, the fur is dark grey brown all over. The muzzle ridge is low with a slight vertical groove. The outer canine width is greater than 5.7 mm and is similar in many respects to N. corbeni, differing primarily on skull morphology. For the purposes of this guide however, the distribution and shape of the muzzle ridge should separate these species in most instances. Gould's Long-eared Bat, N. gouldi, is smaller (forearm length less than 42 mm in sympatric populations) with a proportionally smaller and narrower head, an OCW of less than 5.1 mm.

Roost Habits They roost alone or in pairs in tree hollows and fissures in tree limbs, often in mature or dead Eucalyptus rudis and Melaleuca rhaphophylla. Roosts are occupied for 1 to 2 days with only small distances travelled between roosts. They moved at least 1200 m from their roost to foraging areas.

Habitat They are restricted to the Banksia woodlands, Casuarina and Melaleuca forests and tall eucalypt forests of Karri, Jarrah, Tuart and Marri of the high rainfall areas of south-western Australia. There is an isolated population in woodland at Eyre Bird Observatory on the edge of the Nullarbor Plain.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  In the Banksia woodlands their diet is predominantly beetles. Their flight is slow, fluttery, undulating and agile as the bat slips in and out of the understorey. They ambush their prey from a perch and can hunt for crickets by passive listening for cricket calls without echolocating or seeing them. They commonly glean insect prey and spend part of their time foraging on the ground. The wing loading is higher than other Nyctophilus species implying higher flight speeds. Flight speeds of 6 to 9km/hr have been recorded while foraging.
