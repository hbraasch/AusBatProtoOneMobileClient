Central Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus major tor
Parnaby 2009

 Other Names Nyctophilus timoriensis (part), Nyctophilus major, Greater Long-eared Bat

Description The fur is grey brown all over. The muzzle ridge is low and has a faint vertical groove. It is smaller (forearm 37.6 to 45.3 mm) and lighter (9.5 to 12.7 grams), with a shorter skull (greatest length less than 18.8 mm) and narrower skull (zygomatic width less than 11.2 mm) than N. major (forearm 43.2 to 48.4 mm; weight 11.5 to 17.5 grams; skull length more than 18.8 mm and width greater than 11.6 mm). The N. corbeni is usually larger (forearm 41.3 to 49.4 mm) and heavier (11.8 to 21.0 grams). N. holtorum is usually smaller with a proportionally narrower snout and OCW of less than 5.1 mm. The lesser Long-eared Bat N. geoffroyi is smaller and has a well developed muzzle ridge.

Roost Habits They have been found roosting in tree cavities, in foliage and under loose bark.

Habitat Desert habitats include mallee, open savannah woodland, desert shrublands and spinifex grasslands, heavy eucalypt woodlands and tall woodlands, and it is less common in open woodlands. It has been found on the fringes of the treeless Nullarbor Plain but not extending any distance into it.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Their flight is slow, fluttery, undulating and agile as the bat slips in and out of the understorey. They ambush their prey from a perch, glean from the ground, from bark and from foliage surfaces, they also forage aerially within vegetation. They are thought to spend part of their time foraging on the ground as they have been captured in pitfall traps. Flight speeds of 5 km/hr when foraging and up to 24 km/hr when commuting have been recorded for this species
