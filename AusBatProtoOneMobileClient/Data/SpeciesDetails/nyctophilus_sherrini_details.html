Tasmanian Long-eared Bat
Nyctophilus sherrini
Thomas 1915

 Other Names Nyctophilus timoriensis (part), Greater Long-eared Bat

Description The fur is dark brown on the back and lighter brown on the belly. It is a large bat with a forearm length of 43.9 to 48.0mm. The muzzle ridge is poorly developed being low with only a shallow central depression. The Lesser Long-eared Bat, N. geoffroyi is the only other long-eared bat in Tasmania. It differs by having a well developed muzzle ridge with a Y-shaped groove, light grey-brown fur on the back with a paler belly and smaller size, forearm less than 42 mm.
The Tasmanian Long-eared Bat, Nyctophilus sherrini, is most closely related to the Greater Long-eared Bat N. major, but it has a smaller and differently shaped skull and shorter tooth row. Externally it resembles larger examples of Gould’s Long-eared Bat, N. gouldi from high rainfall areas of mainland Australia, but is distinguished from N. gouldi by the more inflated brain case and proportionately narrower skull.

Roost Habits Tasmanian Long-eared Bats usually roost alone in hollow limbs, fissures in branches, and under dried sheets of bark still attached to the trunks of dead trees. They also form large breeding colonies in tree hollows. They show a preference for trunk or branch roosts in mature forests

Habitat They are most common in moister habitats such as wet sclerophyll and rainforest. They also occur in Blackwood swamps, Acacia melanoxylon forest with 20m canopy and rainforest-like understorey, dense Tea Tree to 7m, coastal Mallee, as well as gullies and creeks in dry sclerophyll forests. They are more commonly associated with tracks than with water.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  They appear to specialize in non-flying insects eating mainly caterpillars (85%) scorpions (5%) and moths. These are captured by gleaning in the understorey. They spend part of their time foraging on the ground and they have been captured in pitfall traps. They have the broadest wings of all the Tasmanian bats and have the slowest flight. Their flight is undulating and agile as the bat slips in and out of the understorey. They are highly maneuverable, flying close to vegetation often in the lowest densest layer of the forest.
