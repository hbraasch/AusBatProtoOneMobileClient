Mangrove Free-tailed Bat
Ozimops cobourgianus
(Johnson, 1959)

Other Names Mormopterus (Ozimops) cobourgianus, Mormopterus cobourgiana , Mormopterus species 5 (pop U & V), Mormopterus loriae cobourgiana, Tadarida cobourgiana and Tadarida loriae. Little Northern Free-tailed Bat, Little Western Free-tailed Bat, Little Scurrying Bat.

Description A small species with a forearm of 35.6mm (32.0–36.1mm) and weight of 6.8–10.5 g. Head and dorsal fur cinnamon brown and sometimes with a frosting of grey brown. Ventral fur cream, or pale yellow. The ventral fur extends well onto the wing to a line extending from the mid humerus to the knee. The fringe of fur on the upper lip is light creamy yellow. Northern Territory forms are slightly darker. Skin of the muzzle and wings is dark brown. Ears triangular with relatively straight leading edge and rounded tips. The bottom of the ear ends in a semicircular antitragus. Fur colour is quite distinctive in this species and separates it from all other Ozimops.

Roost Habits Northern coastal Free-tailed Bats have been found roosting in small spouts and crevices in dead upper branches of grey mangroves Avicennia marina. They emerge early and swarms of up to 100 individuals may be seen flying above the mangrove canopy soon after sunset, later dispersing to forage alone or in pairs.

Habitat They are primarily restricted to mangrove forests, and adjacent forests and woodlands in floodplain areas, monsoon forests along larger waterways and semi-deciduous vine thickets.

Diet, Flight and Foraging The wings are short, narrow and pointed and the flight is fast, direct and agile. They prey upon flying insects above and beside the forest canopy. They can be seen flying through gaps in the forest formed by roads or creeks and providing unobstructed corridors. Like other molossid species they are agile on the ground. In the Top End they were found to eat large numbers of beetles 74%, wingless ants 22% and leaf-hoppers. A specimen collected in the Northern Territory was taken from a group of about ten bats seen flying about a meadow-like opening in the monsoon forest shortly after sunset. The group dispersed soon after. 

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls This species has a characteristic frequency or around 26 - 30kHz, and like many other Ozimops, has two distinct call forms with flat short calls emitted when searching and steeply sloped calls emitted when assessing potential prey.
