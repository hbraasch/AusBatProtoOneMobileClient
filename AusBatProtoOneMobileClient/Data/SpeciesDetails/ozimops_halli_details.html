Cape York Free-tailed Bat
Ozimops halli
(Reardon, McKenzie and Adams, 2014)

Other Names Mormopterus (Ozimops) halli, Mormopterus species 5 (populations S & T), Mormopterus loriae ridei, Mormopterus planiceps ridei and Mormopterus ridei (part).

Distribution. The Cape York Free-tailed Bat, Ozimops halli was known from only 11 specimens when it was described in 2014 and collected from only two disparate locations: near Coen in central Cape York Peninsula and near Normanton in the Gulf Country. More recently (October 2018) we (S.C.,B.T.) captured two females approximately half way between these sites.

Description A small stout species with forearm length of 34.1 mm (31.1 to 35.3 mm) and weight of 9.4 g. (9.0 to 10.3 g). Dorsal fur variable from rich brown to orange brown with grey flecks and only weakly contrasting with lighter ventral fur; yellowish tinge to the fur on the side of the neck. Skin on ears, wings, and muzzle and under the chin very dark brown; ears with half round antitragus. Distinguishable from all other Ozimops by having a short tapering glans penis with a ventral transverse lip at about half the length of the glans body, and with large epithelial spines that cover the glans body dorsally from the head to the base and ventrally from the lip to the base. Two females captured in early October 2018 had a lot of fat around their rump and a very distinctive and abrupt hair line above the tail

Roost Habits
Natural roosts are unknown although likely to be tree hollows.

Habitat
All specimens have been captured by mist nets and over, or very near to, fresh water in woodland habitats. Vegetation ranged from moderately dense eucalypt woodland to very open eucalypt on black soils.

Diet, Flight and Foraging
High fast flying above vegetation. We (S.C.,B.T) saw these bats flying high and fast around a pair of small dams not long after sunset. They did not come down to drink, instead they flew well above our mist nets repeatedly flying back and forth 5 to 6m above the dams, then veering off to fly above the trees and then back over the dams. Several hours later when the night had cooled and the insects were flying lower, the bats returned and we captured them in the nets 2 meters above the water. Scats collected from these individuals contained mainly bugs (Hemiptera) and a small beetle.  At the time there were very few insects available.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls are short (7 - 14ms) FM with a characteristic frequency of 28-33KHz, typically extending from about 40kHz (53kHz, n=1) and ending at about 28 to 29kHz. As with other Ozimops, there are two call types with the second type being relatively flat range: 32 – 29kHz but with similar characteristic freq.
