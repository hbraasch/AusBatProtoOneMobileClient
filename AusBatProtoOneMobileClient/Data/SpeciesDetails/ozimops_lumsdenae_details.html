Northern Free-tailed Bat
Ozimops lumsdenae
(Reardon, McKenzie & Adams, 2014)

Other Names Mormopterus (Ozimops) lumsdenae, Mormopterus beccarii, Tadarida beccarii. Beccari’s Free-tailed Bat, Lumsden’s Free-tailed bat.

Description A thick, muscular and robust body shape. This is the largest species of Ozimops, (forearm 35.2–40.4 mm, mass 11–19.5 g), with all external measures (except D5MP, the middle phalanx on the 5th finger, that determine wing shape) averaging much greater than for other species. The fur is short and bicoloured. On the back, it ranges from rich brown to sandy grey. with paler bases darker tips. The fur is distinctly paler on the belly. The skin on the face, ears and wings is brown to dark grey. The ears are triangular, rounded at tip and not joined together. The skull is sinuate and the first upper premolar is minute. Females have a long clitoral projection.

Roost Habits Northern Free-tailed Bats roost commonly in tree hollows but have also been found in caves in New Guinea. They often roost in the roofs of houses in Queensland and colonies of up to 50 have been recorded. Several of these bats were found roosting in an old horse blanket in a shed and another one was under the topping cap of a telegraph pole. A survey near Kununurra found this species to be widespread in low numbers in all natural habitats but it was not detected in agricultural areas.

Habitat These bats occupy a broad range of habitats from rainforest, river flood plains, tall open forest, savannah woodlands, arid shrub lands and grasslands. They are commonly caught along water courses, lined with river red gums, in arid areas. Across this distribution it is found in drier shrublands and grasslands to rainforest. It occurs in arid and semiarid areas of Western Australia north of 26S, where it favours woodland patches and riparian zones (McKenzie and Bullen 2009).

Diet, Flight and Foraging They have short narrow and pointed wings and fly with very rapid wing beats. In flight they have an interceptor foraging strategy. Flight is fast and straight with gentle curves but no tight or abrupt turns. Horizontal turns are frequent when foraging along a canopy edge. They are manoeuvrable at relatively high speed only, so they prefer to forage in areas relatively free of obstructions, either above the canopy, or beside treetops, cliffs, slopes or hilltops, and along water courses, favouring sites with well developed riparian zones and permanent pools. As they fly over water they come lower and are often caught in mist nets. When commuting to and from feeding grounds they flatten their wings and retract their hairy feet into pouches in the tail membrane’s trailing edge. Although this sacrifices manoeuvrability it further reduces drag, allowing the bats to fly faster and more efficiently. They have been recorded flying at speeds of 28km/hr. Their diet is predominantly aerial moths and beetles, but they also eat bugs, lacewings, flies and grasshoppers. In the Top End stomachs contained predominantly flightless ants (54%). The presence of flightless insects suggests that it captures prey by scurrying on the ground. They are certainly very agile on the ground and can run surprisingly fast.

Echolocation calls are short FM or almost flat with a duration of 5 - 17ms and characteristic frequency of 23 – 25kHz, range 23 – 28kHz.
