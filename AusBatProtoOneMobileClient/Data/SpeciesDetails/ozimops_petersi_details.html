Inland Free-tailed Bat
Ozimops petersi
(Leche, 1884)

Description The fur on the back is bicoloured, usually light grey to light grey-brown with a creamy white base. The fur on the belly is notably paler, with creamy bases and very light-brown tips. The skin on the ears, muzzle and wings is pink to grey. Males have a short penis (less than 3 mm). It is similar to other Ozimops species.The ears are triangular and not joined across the top of the head. The upper lip overhangs the lower lip and bears a fringe of stiff hairs. There is a clinal increase in size of most wing (and cranial) measures from south to north.

Roost Habits Inland Free-tailed Bats have a flat head and body that enables them to squeeze into small roost sites such as cracks and fissures in trees and posts, between roof beams and corrugated iron roofs, and in lengths of water pipe. They most often roost in tree hollows preferring roosts with small entrances. Colonies are usually small, less than 10. They often roost in association with the inland Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens balstoni. Colonies usually have several roosts in an area and move between them every few days.

Habitat This species is common in the more arid parts of Australia, generally associated with open woodland or shrubland and often caught along water courses lined with river red gums. They have been found in cypress pine woodlands, mallee, myall and mulga woodlands, chenopod shrublands and grasslands. They tend to be more common in areas of taller vegetation associated with creek lines and drainage areas.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Stomach contents have included flies, winged and wingless ants. They tend to forage in open unobstructed areas. When foraging over water they were observed to fly 3 to 5m above the surface and then drop rapidly for a few metres to scoop up water before rising again to their previous height. They fly fast in open space both above and below the canopy, over water or along tree-lined creeks where they catch and eat insects while flying. They are fast but not very manoeuvrable in flight. They will sometimes land and crawl rapidly over the ground and on tree trunks chasing and eating prey.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls

Characteristic frequency 29 to 36 kHz (n = 9). Call similar shape to other Ozimops s species with both short, flat calls in search mode and then steeper curved calls when contact is made with potential prey.
