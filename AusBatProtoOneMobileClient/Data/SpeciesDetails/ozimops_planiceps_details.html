Southern Free-tailed Bat
Ozimops planiceps
(Peters, 1866)

 

Description This is a medium-sized Ozimops with a forearm length of 33.8 mm (32.2 to 36.1 mm) and weight of 9.0 g (6.5 to 12.0g). The fur on the back is uniform in colour but varies between individuals from grey-brown to light brown with a pale base. The belly fur is paler brown with a lighter base and tips. The bare skin of the face and wings may be darkly pigmented. The ears are triangular and not joined across the top of the head. The head is distinctly flattened. The most obvious identifying characteristic of this species is only found in males; the penis is unusually long (9 to 11 mm).

Roost Habits These bats roost in tree hollows and the roofs of houses. One colony was found under the metal cap of a power pole. A study of roosts showed that colonies of females are fairly large and average 30 to 40 bats, whereas males form smaller colonies of 3 or 4, although up to several hundred may occur in building roosts. Roosts are often narrow and not much larger than the bats themselves. They often share these roosts with several other species including the Little Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens greyii, Chocolate Wattled Bat, Chalinolobus morio, Large Forest Bat, Vespadelus darlingtoni and the Southern Forest Bat, Vespadelus regulus.

Habitat They have been found in a variety of habitats from tall forests, dry open forest and river red gum Eucalyptus camaldulensis, box-ironwood, box iron bark and cypress pine woodlands, mallee, Mulga, grassland, coastal heathlands and chenopod shrublands. They also occur in some suburban areas across their range.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  These bats typically forage at or above canopy height and in the spaces between the trees, along roadways, the outer edge of remnant vegetation and above the forest canopy. They are rarely observed in large open spaces, such as paddocks devoid of trees. Although treeless areas are used for commuting between foraging areas they may be less important for foraging due to low food abundance. They have also been recorded foraging on the ground, where they are extremely agile. They have difficulty taking off from the ground and will usually climb 1 to 2 m before launching. Radio tracking studies have shown that they forage up to 12 km from their roost. In rural Victoria up to 80% of the diet was bugs (Hemiptera). These were almost exclusively Rutherglen Bugs, Nysius vinitor, a native species that is a sporadic agricultural pest. They are widely distributed and one of the most abundant insects, being available both day and night, and throughout the year. Bugs are an unusual diet and they are rarely eaten by other bat species. Other dietary items included flies, beetles, moths, ants, termites and woodlice.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls
Characteristic frequency 25 to 31 kHz, flat or curved, often transitioning between the two call shapes.
