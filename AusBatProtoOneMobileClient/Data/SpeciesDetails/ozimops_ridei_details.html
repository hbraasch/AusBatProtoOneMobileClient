Eastern Free-tailed Bat
Ozimops ridei
(Felten,1964)

Description A small species with forearm 32.8 mm (30.0 to 35.3 mm) and weight of 9.0 g (6.3 to 11.2 g). The fur on the back is quite variable, from grey to light brown to rich chocolate brown colour with a light creamy brown base to the hairs. The belly fur is only slightly paler than the back. The fur tends to be long and shaggy. The skin of the face, ears and wings is variable colour.The penis length averages 4 mm long, and the glans penis is short, cylindrical and tapered at the tip, with the glans body covered in small epithelial spines.

Roost Habits Eastern Free-tailed Bats roost mainly in tree hollows but will also roost under bark, in buildings and cracks in posts. Colonies of up to several hundred have been recorded. They commonly share roosts with Gould’s Wattled Bats, Chalinolobus gouldii and the eastern Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens orion.

Habitat They have been recorded from a wide range of habitats, including rainforest, mangrove forest, paperbark forest, monsoon forest, tall open forest, River Red Gum (Eucalyptus camaldulensis) and Yellow Box (E. melliodora) woodlands, freshwater wetland, riparian open forest and dry sclerophyll forest. Spotted gum/grey gum/ironbark woodland, inland dry sclerophyll forest of Corymbia citriodora, open forest of C. citriodora and ironbark with scattered Callitris, open woodland of Eucalyptus microcarpa and E. crebra with sparse sub-canopy of Allocasuarina luehmannii and Melaleuca decora. They have been found to avoid urban areas for foraging.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  In Victoria the diet is reported to includ predominantly bugs (60%), with flies (18%), beetles (15%), a few moths, termites, ants and occasional spiders. The bugs were almost exclusively Rutherglen Bugs, Nysius vinitor, a native species that is a sporadic agricultural pest. They are widely distributed and one of the most abundant insects, being available both day and night, and throughout the year. Bugs are an unusual diet and they are rarely eaten by other bat species. Eastern Free-tailed Bats fly in less cluttered environments, usually in spaces between trees or above the canopy but not commonly in large open spaces, such as paddocks devoid of trees, although treeless areas are used for commuting between foraging areas. In Victoria they have a higher level of activity in riparian areas and sites with taller canopies, indicative of this species’ preference for moister environments compared to the Southern Free-tailed Bat, Ozimops planiceps. Thermal biology studies done on tropical Eastern Free-tailed Bats in captivity showed that thy could lower their body temperature to 10oC. This appears to be contrary to the common view that Free-tailed bats cannot hibernate or are poor hibernators

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls s

Characteristic frequency 26 to 36 kHz. Flat or curved, sometimes with down-sweeping tail. Sometimes exhibits on upper harmonic around 53 to 60 kHz.
