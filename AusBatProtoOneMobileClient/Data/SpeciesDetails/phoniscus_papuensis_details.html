Golden-tipped Bat
Phoniscus papuensis
(Dobson, 1878)

Description Considered to be a spider-eating specialist, this bat is distinctively coloured with dark brown curly fur with bright golden tips to each hair. The fur extends along the wings, legs and tail, with abundant hair on the tail membrane. The ears are distinctly funnel shaped with a very long, straight and pointed tragus with a notch at its base. The nose is conspicuously pointed and overhangs the lower jaw. The long, thin upper canines are grooved and fit into pockets in the lower lip. The tail is longer than the combined head and body length and the calcaneum is long, strong and curved backwards.

Roost Habits Although recorded from tree hollows and amongst leaves and epiphytes the vast majority of roosts have been in the (mostly) abandoned nests of Yellow-throated Scrub Wrens and Brown Gerygones. These dome-shaped nests are suspended from thin vines and twigs and made of bark fibre, moss and lichen. All nests used as roosts had a hole in the base made by the bat to facilitate entry. Many of the roosts were occupied by single bats, mainly solitary males. Males also burrow beneath clumps of hanging epiphytic moss to roost. Bats changed roosts every day or two, with a maximum stay of 4 days. In one study, the average distance between roosts was 350 m. Maternity colonies of 5 to 20 bats have been recorded and there is at least one instance where the bats colonised a map hanging up on a verandah. When vegetation immediately adjacent the mop was cleared, the bats abandoned the mop.

Habitat Most captures are in rainforest or rainforest sub-storey with a well developed overstorey of eucalytus or brushbox. The remaining records are from tall open forest, dry and wet sclerophyll forest, Casuarina dominated riparian forest and coastal Melaleuca forests. Many of these forests lack a rainforest sub-canopy. Several individuals have been recorded inside houses on the edge of residential areas.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Orb web spider remains are found in 99% of stomach contents and scat analyses. Small quantities of beetles, moths and traces of flies, bugs and balls of spider web are also ingested. Typically these bats forage within a 2 km radius inside rainforest gullies or cluttered habitat and up the slope into sclerophyll forest, where web spiders are more common. They avoid the more open areas. Web building spiders are present year round but they are more common in the wetter summer months. Spider remains found in the stomach are generally rather intact, suggesting that the bats may suck the spiders dry before swallowing the soft abdomen.

The broad wings and large tail membrane of Golden-tipped Bats allow them to hover and manoeuvre precisely, their broad-band frequency sweep calls are designed to provide fine detail and texture. Both are essential traits for finding spider webs in the dark and plucking out the spiders without getting tangled.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls Steep, near vertical calls, starting at between 160 and 80 kHz, usually around 120 kHz and dropping to as low as 40 kHz, usually between 55 and 85 kHz (n = 18). The call is very soft (low amplitude).
