Mangrove Pipistrelle
Pipistrellus westralis
Koopman, 1984

Description This tiny bat of coastal northern Australia's is our smallest bat. The fur is dark grey tipped with light brown on the back and tipped with buff on the belly. The muzzle, ears and forearms are usually brown to light brown and the wings are dark brown. The ear is broadly triangular and tragus is less than half the length of the ear. The wing joins the foot at the base of the fifth digit. The calcar has an elongated lobe. The head of the glans penis is slightly flared with a central circular depression at the tip, containing many small fleshy spines. The baculum is shorter with a wider base than for P. adamsi and the shaft is straight with a only the final 10% of the tip forked. It differs P. adamsi by its narrower outer canine width; P. westralis is 3.4 to 3.8 mm and P. adamsi is 3.9 to 4.3 mm.

Habitat In Western Australia they appear to be virtually confined to mangroves, but in the Top End they have a broader habitat range, still living primarily in mangroves, but also on floodplains, riverine forests and adjacent woodlands.

Roost Habits They are presumed roost in tree hollows although no roost sites have been reported.

  Diet, Flight and Foraging  These bats forage along small creeks and tidal waterways in mangrove forests they fly 2 to 3 m above the ground. Flight can be relatively direct, but they also circle tightly around bushes and small trees and execute abrupt dives in pursuit of prey .
Main prey items in the diet of P. westralis is reported to include Lepidoptera (23.7%), Coleoptera (29.2%), Hymenoptera (24.7%) and Hemiptera (16.3%). Few family-level identifications were possible in this study. It is unclear if P. adamsi and P. westralis glean when foraging, although there is possibly more circumstantial evidence to support gleaing in P. adamsi than in this species.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls  are curvilinear with a characteristic frequency of 46.6 kHz (44.0 to 49.3 kHz). In the Top End the call overlaps with P. adamsi.
