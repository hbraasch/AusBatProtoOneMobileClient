Black Flying Fox
Pteropus alecto
Temminck, 1837

Description A widespread and common bat of northern and eastern Australia, it is a large species with a forearm length of 153 to 191 mm. The fur is black often with a reddish brown mantle on the back of the neck. The black belly fur is sometimes sprinkled with white tipped hairs giving a frosted appearance. The head is black and some individuals have faint red-brown eye rings. The leg is furred to the knee.

Roost Habits Black Flying Foxes roost in mangroves, rainforest, bamboo, Melaleuca and monsoon forests as well as remnant bushland in urban areas. Camps can be quite small and easily overlooked in tropical Australia, or can be large (30 000 or more) and easily heard from distance away. The same roost site may be used for many years. In southern Queensland large, reproductively important camps are formed throughout summer (September to April), after which juveniles form winter camps locally and adults disperse to distant smaller camps. In the Top End the wet season (summer) camps are in rainforest and in the dry season (winter) the bats move only relatively a short distance (100 km), to camp in bamboo thickets and mangroves, relying on blossom rather than the fruit they lived on in the wet. Roost sites are sometimes established in cave entrances with bats moving in as far as 40m to roost on the cave walls (Chillagoe, Queensland and Tunnel Creek, in Western Australia).

Habitat P. alecto use a wide range of habitats in tropical and sub-tropical woodlands and appear to be moving increasingly south along Australia’s eastern seaboard into more temperate climes. Rainforest, monsoon gallery and open forests, as well as urban amenity plantings, provide a rich source of food when females bear and raise young..

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Individuals will travel up to fifty kilometres from their camp to foraging areas but usually remain within a 20 km radius. Their preferred food is fleshy fruits and the blossoms of eucalypts, Melaleuca and turpentines as well as a variety of other native and introduced blossoms. They have been seen to eat the leaves of trees (Albizia) by chewing the leaves into a bolus, swallowing the liquid and then spitting out the fibre. This provides the bat with most of the protein and nutrients from the leaves without the bulk. They often use the same technique for eating fruit, notably mangoes, spitting out the fibers after extracting the juice. They have also been observed licking the sap from incisions in eucalypt trees made by yellow-bellied gliders in north Queensland (S.C.).

Black Flying Foxes have a sustained flight speed of 25 km per hour. With wind assistance speeds of 60 kms per hour have been recorded. Bats fitted with satellite tracking radio-collars have flown across Torres Strait between New Guinea and Australia, a distance of 150 km.
