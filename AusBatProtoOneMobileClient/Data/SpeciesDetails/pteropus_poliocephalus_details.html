Grey-headed Flying Fox
Pteropus poliocephalus
Temminck, 1825

Description This is the only Australian Flying Fox to have a mantle of rusty brown fur fully encircling the neck. The fur on the back is dark grey although some individuals have a pronounced silver frosting to the hairs. The head is covered with light grey fur and the belly fur is grey, often with flecks of white and ginger. The fur extends down the legs and to the toes. Large males can attain a body weight of 1100 g and a wingspan of 1500 mm.

Roost Habits Grey-headed Flying Foxes will form camps in almost any dense vegetation greater than 3 m in height. Throughout summer these camps may be made up of 200 000 individuals or more, depending on local blossom availability. In winter the young form winter camps while the adults disperse up to 750 km away. Some adults remain in the winter camps when food is plentiful. Camps may be shared with Little Red Flying Foxes, P. scapulatus, throughout their range and with Black Flying Foxes, P. alecto, in the north. They do not move camp as a unit but as individuals or small groups. This causes intermixing of the populations across the whole of their distributional range.

Roosts are also reported to be segregated by sex and age leading up to the mating season. Mature females and immatures roost lower down and more towards the periphery than mature males who establish ‘mating territories’ in the centre of the colony. At that time, male territories may hold harems of up to five receptive females, but the more central, highest-quality males may be joined sequentially by more than 25 females over a single season.

Habitat This species often roost along watercourses in stands of native vegetation such as mangrove, rainforest, Melaleuca, Casuarina, or occasionally in introduced trees. Grey-headed Flying Foxes live much further south than any other megabat and can tolerate a few degrees of frost, though their recent 450 km extension of range to the city of Melbourne has depended on the fact that human activity there has changed the local climate, making it warmer, with less frost and higher rainfall and humidity. The fact that there are 350 000 street trees providing abundant food might also be an important factor. There is also recent evidence that suggests that this species can achieve a degree of torpor, although further research is required. They have a sophisticated vocal communication with over 20 situation-specific calls identified.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Grey-headed Flying Foxes forage at night at up to 50 km from their camp. Their diet consists of the fruit, flowers, pollen, nectar, and in rare cases, leaves of 201 plant species of 50 families. Almost half of these are Myrtaceae, including their major food source, Eucalyptus blossom. They also eat fruits from a variety of tree species particularly native figs (Ficus). They have also been found to chew leaves and appear to eat the salt glands from mangroves. They are known to raid orchards of cultivated fruit, particularly in years with poor blossom abundance. The availability of foraging resources, particularly eucalypt blossom is related to rainfall in a highly complex and still poorly understood way. Spring and summer rainfall is regular in most years, enabling seasonal breeding but extreme droughts, commonly followed by periods of well above average rainfall make food supplies unpredictable. Recently, very large numbers of bats have died during extreme heat events and also possibly as a result of high intensity, widespread bushfires.
