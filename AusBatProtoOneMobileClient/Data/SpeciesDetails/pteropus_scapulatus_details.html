Little Red Flying Fox
Pteropus scapulatus
Peters, 1862

Distribution This species occurs from Shark Bay in Western Australia through the Top End to Queensland and south through New South Wales into northern Victoria. There are occasional records from South Australia. They occur on many islands including Melville, Groote, Torres Strait (Thursday, Horn, Badu, Hammond, Muralug Boigu), Palm, Hinchinbrook and Frazer Islands. This species is more nomadic and has a broader distribution than other Flying Foxes. It is restricted to Australia although it has occasionally been reported on the southern coast of Papua New Guinea and one windblown vagrant was recorded in New Zealand in 1927.

These bats are known to range a long distance inland depending on the availability of flowering trees.

Description A relatively small flying fox with rich reddish-brown to light-brown fur all over its body, often with a grey fur on the head. Some individuals have a yellow patch on the back of the neck and shoulders. The legs are sparsely furred or naked. Wings are red brown and translucent in flight. There are distinct patches of light creamy brown where the wing membrane and the shoulder meet.

Roost Habits Little Red Flying Foxes are nomads, whose movements depend on the availability of blossom, a sporadic food resource. They congregate in large camps of up to one million individuals in early summer. Typically, they hang close together and their combined weight when in large numbers often causes severe damage to their roost trees. They commonly share the camps with other flying foxes. Their camps are more temporary than those of other species usually only lasting for 4 to 6 weeks, and they frequently camp besides water. A colony of 200 000 Little Red Flying Foxes was observed to displace resident Grey-headed and Back Flying Foxes in a permanent camp. They moved into the centre of the camp and pushed the other species to the edges. They also tended to displace resident Flying Fox species from local feeding areas.

Habitat They use a broad range of habitats, from semi-arid areas to tropical and temperate eucalypt forests, paperbark swamps and monsoon forests.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Little Reds feed predominantly on blossom. All dominant tree and shrub species within their range are included in the diet. This resource is largely unpredictable with eucalypt trees producing large amounts of blossom one year and little the next. The flowering time of many Australian plants is very variable and largely dependent on climatic conditions. A nomadic lifestyle enables this bat to utilise this unpredictable food supply. Fruit, sap, and insects are also eaten, and cultivated fruit is eaten when other food resources are not available. They sometimes leave their camp before dark and in northern Australia can be seen foraging during the day during the overcast wet season period. The flight of this species is direct and of moderate speed. They are not very manoeuvrable as evidenced by their crash-landings in trees. They are pretty helpless on the ground and need to climb a tree to become airborne again. In dry years individuals are commonly found entangled in barbed wire fences or drowned in water tanks in the western areas of the species range. Loss of habitat from land clearing and drought conditions will drive them to coastal areas but during wet years they are able to exploit extensive areas of inland Australia. The extent of their migration varies from year to year in terms of numbers of animals, the areas they occupy and the composition of the migrating groups. There is little genetic segregation across the species distribution, suggestive of a single, large interbreeding population.
