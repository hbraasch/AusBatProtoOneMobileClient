Lesser Queensland Horseshoe Bat
Rhinolophus sp.
(undescribed species)

Description This species has long, fine, grey brown fur peppered with white hairs and a lighter belly. The nose-leaf, anus and penis or pubic teats are usually grey. The sella is 5.3 to 5.8 mm wide, less than two thirds of the horseshoe width. It has a forearm length of 50.0 to 53.5 mm and ear length 25.2 to 27.3, weight of 8.3 to 9.9 g.

This species occurs sympatrically with the eastern Horseshoe Bat, R. megaphyllus and the Greater Queensland Horseshoe Bat R. robertsi over a very restricted distributional range on eastern Cape York Peninsula. Although the genetic study (Cooper et al. 1998) failed to resolve the relationships between the three Australian species they differ significantly in body size, nose-leaf shape, ear length and echolocation calls.

Sella width and ear size are excellent features for distinguishing all of the Australian horseshoe bats.

Roost Habits Lesser Queensland Horseshoe Bats have not been captured at the roost and although they have been caught close to mines it is not known if they roost in them. Confusion with the Greater Queensland Horseshoe Bat, R. robertsi that has often been caught in caves and mines has confused the picture. Of the 10 individuals that we have information on, all were caught in forest. They may roost in dense vegetation and tree hollows in more humid locations.

Habitat Rainforest and gallery forest.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  A light-tag attached to a male bat in July showed it foraging within rainforest and around lights at a building in a small clearing. The bat used only continuous flight foraging to capture prey. When the bat roosted in a tree between foraging bouts it was either to rest or to consume prey. There was no evidence of perch hunting. It flew at a height of 0.5 to 2m above the ground, the flight was slow and fluttery with occasional sudden changes in direction to capture insects such as moths on the wing. It was also seen to catch an insect by gleaning from the ground. To reach the building the bat crossed over 200 m of cleared area. Its flight across the grassland was fast and direct and it did not forage.

Echolocation This bat has a constant frequency call of 40 kHz.
