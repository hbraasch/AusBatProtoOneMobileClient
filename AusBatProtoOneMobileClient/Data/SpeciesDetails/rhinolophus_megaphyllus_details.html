Eastern Horseshoe Bat
Rhinolophus megaphyllus
Gray, 1834

Description The only Australian Horseshoe Bat to extend its range to outside the tropics. This bat most commonly has has greyish brown fur, which is slightly lighter on the belly. The fur has white tips and a grey base and is long and fine. In Queensland there is a bright rufous form that may be found roosting among grey individuals. The nose leaves, wings, and ears are pinkish grey.

It can be distinguished from other Rhinolophus by its smaller ears and smaller size. The sella of the nose-leaf is less than one-third the width of the horseshoe.

Roost Habits Eastern Horseshoe Bats are cave dwellers, inhabiting a wide variety of caves and abandoned mines, but are also found in sea caves, rock piles, buildings, old railway tunnels, tree roots in undercut creek banks, stormwater drains and culverts. This species makes use of the widest range of roost sites in the tropics but is more restricted in southern regions, possibly due to the microclimate restrictions. Colonies are typically small, less than 20 bats, but females congregate in maternity colonies of 15 to 2000 bats in spring and summer. A maternity colony of over 10,000 was recently found in a sandstone cave near Sydney. To rear their young they select warm and humid caves (85 to 95% relative humidity), with little air circulation. In temperate latitudes these bats disperse in winter and are found roosting singly in the cooler parts of caves in a torpid state. In the tropics they are active and forage nightly throughout the year. On Cape York Peninsula, they have been found in colonies of several hundred to several thousand during the winter dry season. These are not maternity sites, but large aggregations of both sexes in non-breeding condition.

When roosting, they cling to the ceiling by their toes, hanging free with their wings wrapped around their body. When approached they swivel, often hanging by one leg and flicking their ears alternately while echolocating to examine what caused the disturbance.

Habitat A broad range of habitats are used - tropical and temperate rainforest, deciduous vine forest, dry and wet sclerophyll forest, open woodland, coastal scrub and grassland areas. Being adapted to forage in clutter, they are much more active in mature forests with dense understorey, and commonly forage along tracks and waterways, avoiding large cleared areas such as paddocks.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  They are well adapted to foraging in cluttered habitats, having short broad wings and low wing loading, giving them a slow but highly manoeuvrable flight. They often hover and they are able to manoeuvre successfully among the branches and foliage of dense shrubs. Both continuous flight and perch hunting are utilised, prey being captured by aerial pursuit and gleaning. Moths (mainly non-eared species) are the dominant food.
