Queensland Horseshoe Bat
Rhinolophus robertsi
Tate, 1952

Description An extraordinary looking bat with enormous ears and nose-leaf. It has long, fine, grey brown fur peppered with white hairs and a lighter belly. The nose-leaf has a large sella more than two-thirds the width of the horseshoe. The nose-leaf, anus and penis or pubic teats may be either grey or bright yellow.

The size of this species varies with latitude (forearm averages 55.8; 52.3 to 59 mm) becoming smaller in the south. In the northern part of its range (north of Coen) where the three species overlap in distribution it is considerably larger and heavier with a forearm length of 56 to 59 mm and ear length of 32.1 to 33.3 mm, weight 11.5 to 16.2 g. Sella width is a highly reliable diagnostic character.

Roost Habits One individual was captured in a disused mine at Iron Range where it was roosting alone hanging free from the end of a small ledge on a wall. The roost conditions were 26oC and 78% relative humidity. It was sharing a roost with several hundred eastern Horseshoe Bats, R. megaphyllus. and could be distinguished amongst this large colony by its large ears and larger size. A further 6 were seen in a mine at Mt Amos. They were roosting alone, hanging from the ceiling, scattered through the mine. The roost conditions were 26.5oC with 98% humidity. The mine was flooded waist deep in water at the time and contained several thousand, eastern Long-fingered bats, M. o. oceanensis and several hundred eastern Horseshoe Bats, R. megaphyllus. Eighteen individuals were collected from this mine (the type locality) in 1950.
It is likely that this species also roosts in culverts, under eroded creek banks and in large hollow eucalypts, but further investigation is required.

Habitat Greater Queensland Horseshoe Bats have been found in lowland rainforest, along gallery forest lined creeks within open eucalypt forest, Melaleuca forest with rainforest understorey, open savannah woodland, tall riparian woodland of Melaleuca, E. tereticornis and E tesselaris and at the edge of a grassy clearing in the rainforest.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  Faecal pellets collected from pregnant females in late November contained predominantly moths and beetles with some grasshoppers and crickets. These bats foraged using continual flight, not perch hunting. Their flight was slow and fluttery with sudden changes of direction and height. They flew below canopy height between 1 and 8 m above the ground within rainforest that grew both above and along the edge of a road. I have watched this bat catch a large moth near a light in an open house. It roosted in the dark corner of the room to eat its meal, discarding the wings before flying out again (S.C.).

Echolocation and Vocalisations The echolocation call of R robertsi is at a constant frequency of 27 to 30 kHz which distinguishes it from other Rhinolophus species.
