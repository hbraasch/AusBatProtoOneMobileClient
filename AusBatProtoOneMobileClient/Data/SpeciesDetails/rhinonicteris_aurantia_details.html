Orange Diamond-faced Bat
Rhinonicteris aurantia
(Gray, 1845)

Description One of our most attractive bats! Most individuals are yellowish-cream to mid golden brown, while variations on this scheme include bats with dark brown or dark grey dorsal surfaces and creamy white undersides. Often these bats have dark fur around the eyes. The most reliable character for identification is the diamond-shaped noseleaf and its foliations. Forearm is 45.2 – 55.2 mm.

Roost Habits Orange Diamond-faced Bats are dependent on caves and mines with very hot and humid microclimates (28 to 32oC and 96% to 100% relative humidity), during the tropical dry season. Caves and mines with these conditions are uncommon. These bats roost hanging free from the ceiling or against the cave wall and separated from each other by 10 to 15 cm. Physiological and behavioural characteristics explain the dependence on these microclimates: Orange Diamond-faced Bats cannot enter torpor or maintain body temperature for prolonged periods when exposed to low temperatures, and individuals do not cluster for warmth. Colony size varies from 5 to over 20,000. Bats leave the all the dry season roost caves from November to February, during the wet season, when the outside climatic conditions are similar to the dry season roost microclimate. They have not been found in caves at this time but are often encountered in buildings and other similar structures. It is likely that they become forest dwellers at this time and forage over a much greater range. These bats are very susceptible to dehydration and hypothermia. Their rates of evaporative water loss have been found to be more than double that of any other bat. Removed from the high temperature and humidity of their cave roosts they die within hours. For this reason, they are difficult to hold in captivity and will die in bat traps if these are not checked frequently. They are known to abandon roost caves if disturbed repeatedly.

Pilbara population a good deal of research has been done on the geographically isolated Pilbara population. The main difference between the populations is that the echolocation call is a higher frequency (121kHz compared to 114.6kHz for northern bats) and the bats are slightly smaller, but their morphology and basic biology is the same. Much of this research has been to examine the effect of Iron-ore mining operations on bat populations and several new colonies have been found. Monitoring has shown that colonies remain at their roost cave location in the face of closely adjacent mining operations as long as the temperature and humidity conditions within the caves remain unaffected. Like their northern Australian counterparts, Pilbara Orange Diamond-faced Bats disperse in the wet season when foraging and roosting opportunities increase and return to the preferred primary roosts during the dry season. They do not abandon primary roosting caves when the area has been burnt by fire as long as the local water resource is not affected, and presumably the local insect populations remain in place at the water resource. Unlike the Ghost Bat, Macroderma gigas that leave the area and then return as the undergrowth regenerates. Recent research has shown that even in the harsh conditions of the Pilbara, they are capable of traveling a long distance between roosts. An individual marked with a pit tag was later encountered at a roost 170 km away.

Habitat Orange Diamond-faced Bats are found in monsoon rainforest, tall open forest, open savannah woodland, black soil grassland and spinifex covered hills. They are more influenced by the availability of suitable roost caves than habitat types.

Diet, Flight and Foraging  They are opportunistic in their diet, eating primarily moths and beetles throughout the year but during the wet season they prefer flying termites. The termites provide a fat rich diet for the pregnant and lactating females. Over the year their diet comprised of 70% moths, 17% beetles, 8% termites, with occasional ants, wasps, mantids, lacewings, bugs, flies and cockroaches. They have a rapid flight (speeds up to 26 km/hr) with a fast wing beat and tend to forage with a zigzag flight low to the ground. They are often seen flying along roads at night and their bright orange fur is very distinctive in car headlights.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls are a constant frequency and in the range of 114 to 121 kHz.
