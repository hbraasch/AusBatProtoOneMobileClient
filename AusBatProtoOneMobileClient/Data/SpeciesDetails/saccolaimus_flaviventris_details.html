Yellow-bellied Sheath-tailed Bat
Saccolaimus flaviventris
(Peters, 1867)

Description A striking looking bat, it is large, with rich shiny black fur on the back and contrasting bright white, cream or yellow fur on the belly. Males have a large throat pouch, with a small secondary pouch. There is no pouch in females, instead there is a naked area and a ridge of skin. There is no wing pouch in either sex. The inner edge of the tragus is evenly concave, rounded but with a deep notch just below the top of the outer edge.

Recent work has shown that it can be difficult to distinguish from the Naked-rumped Sheath-tailed Bat, S. saccolaimus. However outer canine width (OCW) is usually larger (5.9 to 7.1 mm) than in S. saccolaimus (5.0 to 6.2 mm). The length of the first phalanx of the 3rd digit of the wing (3P1) may also separate these species (31.7 versus 29.0).

Roost Habits Yellow-bellied Sheath-tailed Bats roost in large tree hollows and are often solitary but may be found in mixed-sex groups of two to six and occasionally large maternity colonies of over 100. In northern New South Wales they were found to roosting in a live Eucalyptus albens tree that had partially lost its crown and had multiple dead limbs. The roost was 9 to 10 m above the ground and the entrance hollows were at the end of rotten tree spouts, where a branch had broken off. The entrances were about 12 cm in diameter.

They have also been found sharing hollows with Northern Free-tailed Bats, Chaerephon jobensis and in the abandoned nests of Sugar Gliders, Petaurus breviceps. Solitary animals have been found roosting in animal burrows, in cracks in dry clay and under slabs of rock in the NT.

Habitat They occur in almost all habitats; from wet and dry sclerophyll forest, open woodland, Acacia shrublands, mallee, grasslands and desert. They are only reported from southern Australia between January and April where they migrate during the summer. Sick or apparently exhausted animals have occasionally been found hanging from the outside walls of buildings in broad daylight in Victoria and South Australia. It is very important not to touch these exhausted or compromised individuals, as this species is a known vector for Australian Bat Lyssavirus.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. They fly fast and straight usually well above the canopy, but lower over open spaces and at the forest edge. In pursuit of insects, they are capable of tight lateral turns. They eat predominantly beetles but also grasshoppers, crickets, leafhoppers. shield bugs, wasps and a few flying ants. In the Top End they are usually only active until midnight but they have been recorded foraging all night on Cape York Peninsula during the wet season. A flight speed of 29km/hr has been recorded WA.

The conspicuous white belly is easily distinguished at night with a spotlight, although both S. mixtus and S. saccolaimus can also appear to be very similar.
Vocalisations and Echolocation calls  Characteristic frequency of 17.5 to 22.5 kHz (n = 21). Usually curved in search phase but may be flat. Often displays harmonics in the 28-33 kHz range and less commonly in the 9 to 13 kHz range. Occasional non- harmonic pulses within a sequence may have characteristic frequency as low as 15 kHz or as high as 24 kHz.
