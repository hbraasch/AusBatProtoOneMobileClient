Papuan Sheath-tailed Bat
Saccolaimus mixtus
(Troughton, 1925)

Description An elusive species that is rarely caught. The fur on the back is made up of a mixture of dark brown and white hairs (60% brown and 40% white) giving an overall grizzled grey appearance. The belly fur varies from white to pale buffy brown with a very small scattering of brown hairs.

Similar species. This species is distinctly smaller (forearm length 62.0 to 68.0 mm) than the other two Saccolaimus. There is a well-developed wing pouch filled with white hairs. There is a strongly developed throat pouch in males, only slightly less developed in females. The rump is well furred. The tragus is slightly thickened, rounded, with the lower inner margin concave, sloping gradually upward to form an obliquely rounded top. Outer canine width is smaller (4.5 to 4.8 mm) than S. saccolaimus (5.0 to 6.2 mm) and much smaller than S. flaviventris (5.9 to 7.1 mm). The length of the first phalanx of the 3rd digit of the wing (3P1) also separates these species (23.7 versus 29.0 and 31.7 mm respectively). Although seen foraging with Yellow-bellied Sheath-tailed Bats there is a difference in their flight morphology. The aspect ratio of S. mixtus is 9.25 compared to 8.25 for S. flaviventris, with a wing loading of 13.6 compared to 15.85. This indicates that Papuan Sheath-tailed Bats probably fly faster and are slightly more manoeuvrable than yellow-bellied Sheath-tailed Bats.

Roost Habits In Australia, Papuan Sheath-tailed Bats roost in tree hollows. One roost containing several individuals was found in the hollow spout of a solitary Eucalyptus on a black soil floodplain. The roost was 5 m above the ground. One specimen in New Guinea was reported to have been captured in a limestone cave

Habitat This species occurs in tall open forests particularly Eucalyptus tetradonta forests, low open woodlands, along creek lines, black soil floodplains and heathlands.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. They have been shown to eat mainly beetles (71%) and large numbers of flying ants (17%) as well as grasshoppers (6%), bugs (4%) and occasional moths (2%) during the wet season. Little is known of their flight and foraging habits. They were seen foraging with yellow-bellied Sheath-tailed Bats high above an isolated homestead where they were feeding on the large numbers of insects attracted by the lights. When the lights were turned off at midnight these bats foraged lower and were caught in a mist net set 10 m above the ground at canopy height. They have also been shot while foraging along a creekline in semi-open heathland. Other specimens have been captured in low open eucalypt woodland in bat traps set along tracks at ground level.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls Characteristic frequency 22-25 kHz with almost-flat to curvilinear and sinuous pulses of variable duration. Not easily separated from other Saccolaimus species.
