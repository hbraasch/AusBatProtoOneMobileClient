Bare-rumped Sheath-tailed Bat
Saccolaimus saccolaimus
(Temminck, 1838)

Description A large, high-flying bat that is rarely caught. It has a forearm length of 75.7mm (71.9 to 80 mm), weight 50g (31.0 to 64.5 g). The fur colour varies between individuals. On the back, fur may be brown or dark reddish brown or almost black, usually with white flecks or speckles, at least on the head and shoulders, although these are absent in juveniles and some adults. The belly fur is also variable from pure white to grey brown. There is a throat pouch in males, and females have a well-defined naked area encircled with a cresent-shaped rudimentary pouch ridge. There is no wing pouch.

Similar species. Some individuals appear almost identical to the Yellow-bellied Sheath-tailed Bat, S. flaviventris and indeed many specimens have been recently discovered mislabelled in Museums. The two species can usually be separated by outer canine width. OCW and presence or absence of white flecking on the fur. If OCW is less than 5.7 mm and/or white flecks are present it is S. saccolaimus. If OCW is greater than 6.0 mm and there is no white flecking it is S. flaviventris. If OCW is between 5.7 and 6.0 mm and there are no white flecks then it can only be identified by DNA (analysis). Bare-rumped Sheath-tailed Bats are much larger than the Papuan Sheath-tailed Bat, S. mixtus, OCW of 4.5 to 4.8 mm, forearm length 64.4mm (61.5 to 68.0 mm), weight 23.3g (21.3 to 26.5 g) which also differs in having wing pouches.

The name Bare-rumped Sheath-tailed Bat is based on observations in the literature that the rump, hind legs and feet are hairless, with the fur stopping abruptly in a distinct line between the hips. Recent work has shown that the rump is no less furred than that found in any other Australian sheath-tailed bat. The common name although inappropriate is used throughout its wide distributional range.
 
Roost Habits Bare-rumped Sheath-tailed Bats roost in tree hollows. Usually large old trees with hollow trunks and/or hollow dead branches. In Queensland they have been found the hollow trunks and branches of poplar gums (Eucalyptus platyphylla) in colonies of three to four bats. One colony of ten to 15 bats was found roosting in the large hollow trunk of a dead Darwin Stringybark (Eucalyptus tetradonta), near Iron Range. On Magnetic Island in August 2015 a colony of 50 bats were discovered when a roost tree fell down. The tree containing the roost was a large (~18 m tall), completely hollow, Moreton Bay Ash (Corymbia tessellaris) that had been dead for many years, The bats appeared to only occupy the uppermost four meters of one of the hollow spouts. The hollow in this section was 12 to 20 cm internal diameter and the entire spout was 6 m long.

In the Northern Territory, in April 1996 a colony of about 40, containing adults and large suckling young were found in a hollow of a large Darwin Woollybut (E. miniata) when it was cut down. In December 2006, 130 bats were found in a large hollow (25 cm wide) in a Darwin Stringybark (Eucalyptus tetradonta) that was blown down in a storm. The colony contained adults, sub-adults, large juveniles and very young juveniles. Roosts have also been found in Weeping Paperbark (Melaleuca leucadendron). Colonies size varies from 3 to 130.

Habitat They have been found in a wide range of habitats from humid to semi-arid. From rugged sandstone and limestone uplands, ranges and gorges, as well as volcanic hill country and granite Islands. The vegetation ranges from tropical rainforest, tropical woodland of Poplar Gum (E. platyphylla) and tall open forests of Darwin Stringybark (E. tetradonta) and Darwin Woolybut (E. miniata), gallery forest, Melaeluca swamps, in savannah woodland, Pandanus woodland fringing the floodplains, in grassy beach dunes with Melaleuca and Acacia adjacent to open eucalypt forest, in sandstone plateau country with permanent fresh water, low open woodland of Eucalyptus cliftoniana,, Owenia vernicosa, Terminalia ferdinandiana, and Xanthostemon paradoxu, Melaleuca woodland associated with coastal sand dunes, mangroves, pindan on sand plains and riparian forests.

Diet, Flight and Foraging They feed above the canopy on aerial insects. They have been observed flying from 80 m above the canopy to within 2 m of the ground while swooping down to chase prey. They are fast fliers and surprisingly maneuverable. In Sarawak I (S. Churchill) have watched them leave their roost in a thatched roof before dark. They dropped from the top of the roof for a distance of 3 m, before fully opening their wings and swooping up into the sky. They have been observed foraging within meters of the canopy in riverine gallery forest and Melaleuca dominated swamps in Queensland. They are known to fly at altitudes above 400 m and are likely capable of moving long distances. At Cape Tribulation, North Queensland, they have been identified by their distinctive echolocation call and then tracked and visually observed commuting at dusk and dawn from their roost in lowland tropical rainforest, near the top of a ridge about 150 m above sea level and 1 km inland, to where they were foraging along a narrow coastal strip. They have been observed flying and regularly foraging just above canopy height along a 2. km section of mangrove and littoral forest immediately adjacent to the beach. Foraging bats have been tracked in another 10 locations from areas just north of Cape Tribulation, to as far south as the Daintree River, and up to 15 km inland.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls
In Queensland they were found to make high–pitched calls at the roost that could be heard over 8 m away. In New Guinea they have been noted to make a high-pitched audible clicks during echolocation calls while in flight.
At roosts the species sometimes utters just audible, high-pitched calls when disturbed (Murphy 2002). The recent discovery of an alternating duplet or triplet calling pattern used by foraging individuals, with most energy in the 11- 25 kHz band, is diagnostic for this species. Further studies show that variations of this same unusual calling pattern are seen in Australia, Indonesia (Java), Malaysia (Sarawak and Sabah) and Brunei.

Recent studies have found this species to be widespread throughout the Kimberley of Western Australia. At an overall average frequency of 20.3 kHz, these sequences (like their NT counterparts), are significantly different from S. flaviventris and C. jobensis in all spectral parameters measured, and consistent with (although 2.8 kHz lower than) S. saccolaimus sequences from Queensland.
