Little Broad-nosed Bat
Scotorepens greyii
(Gray, 1843)

Description Abundant in the north, this species has a small, slender body shape. The fur is markedly bicoloured. Fur colour is variable from brown to grey-brown on the back with the base of the hairs lighter than the tips, the belly fur is lighter. The forearm is small (27.3 to 35.0 mm) Ears are relatively broad and the tragus has a narrow and pointed tip. Forearm length increases from east to west and from north to south (the opposite to S. sanborni). The glans penis has up to 10 spines on the head, mainly in two long rows.

It is almost impossible to separate in the field from the northern Broad-nosed Bat, S. sanborni, where their ranges overlap in the Kimberley and western Top End although specimens can be separated by examination and measurement of the skull and by plotting braincase width against palatal length (see Kitchener and Caputi 1985). In these areas genetic analysis is the only reliable method of identification of live animals. Where their ranges overlap in Queensland the northern Broad-nosed Bat, S. sanborni (forearm length 33.3 mm, 31 to 35.8 mm) is averages larger than the little Broad-nosed Bat, S. greyii where the most reliable means of identification is via echolocation call frequency.

Roost Habits Little Broad-nosed Bats roost in hollows, usually in trees but the hollow centres of fence posts and even the space under the metal topping caps of telegraph poles are accepted. They will also roost in disused buildings. Colonies of 2 to 20 bats have been found. Although a common species little has been recorded of its natural history.

Habitat they are found in monsoon forest, Melaleuca forest, tall open forest, open forest, open woodland, mulga shrublands, mixed shrubland, escarpment, river red gum lined watercourses, Pandanus and grasslands. They are commonly caught near water. Dry grassland, woodland, sandy deserts, inland rivers, monsoon forest, paperbark swamps. Roost in tree hollows, buildings, under telegraph pole caps and inside hollow fence posts.

Diet, Flight and Foraging.  This species is a continuous flight forager that has a moderately fast, agile flight. They primarily search for insects close to the tree tops but not above them, flying in the open spaces along the contour of the vegetation within 2 meters of the foliage. They also forage over water, grasslands and other open habitat. Flight is characterised by abrupt horizontal turns where the bat rolls to near vertical bank angles. They eat large numbers of beetles, bugs and ants with occasional moths, termites, cockroaches, katydids, crickets, bugs, flies and lacewings. The high proportion of flightless insects such as ants in the diet indicates that they may glean food from foliage and other surfaces. Michael Pennay has watched these bats near Bourke in New South Wales, dog-fighting and catching moths almost as large as themselves. They are tenacious bats with a surprisingly strong bite.  A flight speed of 22km/hr recorded in WA.
Vocalisations and Echolocation calls  Characteristic frequency between 35 and 41.5 kHz (n = 14). Curved, tail usually up-sweeping but may be down-sweeping or absent in some pulses. Sometimes gives flatter pulses.
