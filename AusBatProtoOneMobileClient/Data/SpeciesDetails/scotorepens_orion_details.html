Eastern Broad-nosed Bat
Scotorepens orion
(Troughton, 1937)

Description This robust bat is of moderate size, forearm length 32.4 to 38.8 mm. The fur colour is a rich dark brown on back, with the belly fur more drab. The fur is not markedly bicoloured. It has a pug-nose, the ears are relatively broad and the tragus tip is narrow and pointed. The forearm tends to be longer in the colder areas of this species’ range.

It is most likely to be confused with the inland Broad-nosed Bat, S. balstoni, but their ranges do not overlap except in south-eastern Queensland and along the west of the Great Dividing Range in New South Wales. In this area the ratio of tibia/forearm length is less than .41 for S. orion. This does not always hold true but is a useful character in many cases. S. orion can be separated by the glans penis that has 8 spines on the head in an almost circular cluster. The un-named Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens species (Parnaby) differs from S. orion by its smaller size (forearm less than 32 mm) and weight (6 to 8 gm versus 8 to 12 gm).

Roost Habits Eastern Broad-nosed Bats roost in tree hollows. One roost was found in the hollow of a manna gum, seven metres above the ground. A colony of pregnant females were found sharing a roost in a hollow limb with a colony of chocolate Wattled Bats, Chalinolobus morio, and their young. They have also been reported to roost in buildings.
 
Habitat They have been recorded from rainforest, tall wet forest, vine forest, low open forest, cypress pine woodland, stringy bark and mixed species woodland. Most often in tall wet forest or rainforest on east of Great Dividing Range, also open forests on the western slopes. 

Diet, Flight and Foraging. Nothing known

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls  Characteristic frequency between 34 and 37.5 kHz (n = 21). Curved, tail absent sometimes down- sweeping. Frequency of the knee is usually > 38 kHz.
