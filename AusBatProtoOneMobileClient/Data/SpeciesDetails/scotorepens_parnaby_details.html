Parnaby’s Broad-nosed Bat
Scotorepens species (Parnaby)
(undescribed)

Description Fur colour is brown with a bi-coloured pale belly. In some areas of New South Wales, it coexists with both S. greyii and S. orion. In south-eastern Queensland it occurs with S. greyii and S. balstoni. Although ranges of measurements and weights overlap between these species, live animals from the same area are recognisably distinct

The fur is paler than S orion, and the muzzle is less pug-like. This species is of intermediate body size for the genus, with a mean forearm length of 32.8 mm (31.0 to 34.6) averaging larger than S. greyii, 31.3 mm (27.3 to 35.0) and smaller than both S. orion, 35.1 mm (32.4 to 38.8) and S. balstoni, 35.6 mm (32.0 to 40.5). Body is less robust, average weight of 7.4 g (6.1 to 9.0), than S. orion 9.3 g (7.0 to 14.1) and S. balstoni 9.3 g (6.3 to 12.7), and slightly larger than S. greyii. Ratio of tibia to forearm length is usually greater than .41 in Scotorepens sp. compared to less than .41 for S. orion, but both S. greyii and S. balstoni have ratios that range above and below this figure.

This species differs on skull, dental and external morphology from S. balstoni and S. orion and differs from S. greyii on skull morphology. The details are yet to be published.

Roost Habits An individual was captured and radio-tracked in dry sclerophyll forest on the New South Wales coast. The roost was found in a mature swamp mahogany tree (Eucalyptus robusta) over 100 cm in diameter. The bat was roosting in a hollow branch about 20 m above the ground. They have also been found roosting in buildings, in wall cavities, between floor joists and in an attic.

Habitat Occurs in drier forest types east of the Dividing Range south to the Hunter Valley. It is relatively common west of the Dividing Range in dry open eucalypt woodland and forest and brigalow-belah forests. It has been recorded from Eucalyptus pilligaensis woodland with open Allocasuarina and Callitris; and open forest of Corymbia citriodora and ironbark with scattered Callitris.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. The central-eastern Broad-nosed Bat has a relatively fast flight and forages by aerial pursuit above the canopy or within the sub-canopy. Nothing is known of its diet.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls. Difficult to distinguish from the little Broad-nosed Bat, S. greyii. Characteristic frequency between 38 and 41 kHz (n = 22). Curved, tail usually up-sweeping.
