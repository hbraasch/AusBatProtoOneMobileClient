Northern Broad-nosed Bat
Scotorepens sanborni
(Troughton, 1937)

Description This small, slender species is less aggressive than other Scotorepens. The fur is markedly bicoloured, and brown with a light reddish tinge on the back with a dark brown base. Fur on the belly is slightly lighter. The muzzle is dark. The ears are relatively slender and the tragus is evenly curved upwards. The small forearm (27.9 to 35.8 mm), increases in size from west to east and from south to north (the opposite to S. greyii). The glans penis has up to 10 spines on the head, mainly in two long rows.

It is almost impossible to separate in the field from the little Broad-nosed Bat, S. greyi, where their ranges overlap in the Kimberley and western Top End although specimens can be separated by examination and measurement of the skull and by plotting braincase width against palatal length (see Kitchener and Caputi 1985). In these areas genetic analysis is the only reliable method of identification of live animals. Where their ranges overlap in Queensland S. sanborni (forearm length 33.3 mm; 31 to 35.8 mm) is usually larger than S. greyii (forearm length 31.1 mm; 27.3 to 35 mm).

Roost Habits Northern Broad-nosed Bats usually roost in tree hollows in colonies of 10 to 20 bats but they have also been found in large colonies of several hundred in buildings.

Habitat In Queensland they occur in a broad range of habitats from monsoon forest to open woodland and heathlands. They have not been found in patches of monsoon forest in the Kimberley or Top End, preferring mangrove communities and floodplain areas adjacent to paperbark forest. Understories of coastal forests. Favour watercourses, edges of quiet coastal bays, billabongs alongside monsoon forest and urban areas. Roosts in tree hollows, houses or sheds.

Diet, Flight and Foraging.  In Western Australia they forage cluttered air spaces within the canopy and alongside mangroves. In Queensland they fly within the forest understorey usually below 4 to 5 m above the ground. In the Top End beetles (44%), bugs (28%) and ants (18%) were the main prey items with occasional moths, termites, cockroaches and lacewings. Crane flies, midges, mosquitos and mayflies have been reported elsewhere from examining stomach contents. Speed of 22km/hr recorded in WA.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls Characteristic frequency 38.0 kHz.
