Bristle-faced Free-tailed Bat
Setirostris eleryi
(Reardon and McKenzie 2008)

Description The Bristle-faced Free-tailed Bat is a small, lightly built species, weighing 5.1 g (4.0 to 6.0 g), with a forearm length of 33.6 mm (31.5 to 35.9 mm). It has a long thin face with about 30 scattered dark bristles and a fringe of fur around the top lip. The muzzle is much narrower than Ozimops species with an OCW of 3.5 mm (3.0 to 3.8 mm), the face tapers rapidly to the enlarged nostrils. The chin falls well short of the snout. There is no throat (gular) sac in either sex. The ears are triangular in shape and with the anterior edge straight. The ears are not joined across the forehead but separated by a gap about 2 mm. There is a small antitragus and the tragus is rounded. The fur is brownish grey to pale sandy, to reddish brown on the back with the bases to the fur darker grey and with very light frosting on the tips. The fur on the belly is pale cream to white. Toes each have three long hairs extending at least the length of the toes. Both males and females have a long slander fleshy finger-like projection arising from the genitals. (This feature is also found in the East-coast Free-tailed Bat, Micronomus norfolkensis). In males it arises from the tip of the dorsal surface or foreskin of the penis and is almost as long as the penis. In females it arises from the upper edge of the genital opening. There are hairs along the projection with several hairs extending well beyond the tip. Males also possess penis is that is flattened and is acutely pointed at the tip. The glans penis is unique in having serrated lateral flanges,with the serrated flanges becoming wider at the base.

Roost Habits A radio tracking study in New South Wales found several roosts in eucalypts (Eucalyptus populnea and E intertexta) amongst the fringing vegetation of a dry creek. The roosts were in trunk and branch hollows with entrances only just big enough for one bat to squeeze through (1.3 to 2 cm) at a height of 3 to 6 m above the ground. One roost contained a maternity colony of 15 lactating females. The two males roosted in colonies of 4 and 20 bats (including bats of other species). Roosts were 3.2 to 4 km from the capture sites and two roosts were used on consecutive nights.

Habitat Habitats include open forests of Bimbil Box (Eucalyptus populnea), Belah (Casuarina pauper) and Coolibah (Eucalyptus coolibah) open woodland, River Red gum (Eucalyptus camaldulensis) lined watercourses, Mulga and hummock grasslands. Also with E. polyanthemos, E. melanophoia and Corymbia citriodora in woodlands and forest in the eastern part of the distribution. They have been captured in mist nets and bat traps along ephemeral watercourses and floodways, over dams and waterholes, one was found drowned in a swimming pool.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. The Bristle-faced Free-tailed Bat has a distinctive flight pattern. Their relatively long narrow wings suggest straight high speed flight but the bats were observed to use a more agile foraging strategy with most foraging relatively close to the ground and near vegetation. Flight was relatively slow, fluttery, and below canopy height at around 3 to 4 metres elevation. They flew in loose circles with occasional rapid changes in height and direction in pursuit of prey.

Although considerably less agile than the Little Broad-nosed Bat, Scotorepens greyii and Gould's Wattled Bat, Chalinolobus gouldii. In energy terms, it hunts and commutes more economically than either species because of its above-average aspect ratio (7.8) and features of its aerodynamics. The restricted agility implies that S. eleryi must detect its prey at a greater distance than the two vespertilionid bats. It is likely that, rather than increasing its echolocation call pulse intensity, and therefore its food requirements, S. eleryi tunes its search-mode pulses more finely to gain the extra detection range. They are optimised to hunt in semi-cluttered air spaces, can commute long distances between insect patches cheaply and it can use flat-wing aerodynamics to reduce profile drag in high-speed flight. Its call repetition rates imply that it has a steady level foraging-flight speed of 23 km per hour. Their diet has not been recorded.

Vocalisations and Echolocation calls. The echolocation call differs from all Australian Mormopterus species calls by having strongly frequency modulated search phase pulses shaped like a reverse ‘J’ compared with more flat shaped pulses of all other Molossid species and is more like a Vespertilionid call. The call of S. eleryi has a characteristic frequency of 37kHz.
