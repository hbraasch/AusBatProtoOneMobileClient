Southern Blossom Bat
Syconycteris australis
(Peters, 1867)

 

Description Like a miniature Flying Fox, its fur is fawn to reddish-brown, slightly lighter on the belly. It has large eyes and a long thin muzzle with raised nostrils, and a very long brush-tipped tongue. It is small with a forearm 40.9 (38.6 to 42.8) mm and weight 18.0 (13.7 to 23.0) grams.

It is only slightly larger and very similar to the Least Blossom Bat, Macroglossus minimus, but differs in having the outer pair of lower incisor teeth larger than the inner pair with no gap between them and lacking the skin flaps along the inside of the legs. There is a strip of long fur in this area instead and there is no tail. Males lack the large scent gland found on the chest of Least Blossom Bats. Individuals in New South Wales are considerably heavier than those in north Queensland.

Roost Habits They roost most commonly in rainforest, most commonly in the sub-canopy but occasionally in the canopy. Roosts are amongst large leaves (sometimes dead), often on the growing tips of saplings or amongst dense vines. They roost with their wings wrapped around them and look like a cluster of dead leaves. the illusion is enhanced by the bats habit of occasionally rocking from side to side as if the wind has disturbed them. They also roost in wet sclerophyll forests where their favoured roosts were in cabbage palm leaves (Livistonia australis). They usually select a different roost every night but stay in the same vicinity (within 50 m) for the season. During the winter they roost near the forest edge, but in summer they select roosts deep within the forest where it is cooler. They have a high energy demands and tend to enter torpor most days.

Habitat They are found from sea level to about 3,000 m. In northern New South Wales, the combination of heathland and coastal rainforest is essential for this species. They have a foraging area of about 13 ha of heathland and use the same area each night. Further north along the Queensland coast they live in rainforest, wet sclerophyll, monsoon and Melaleuca forests.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. They are specialist nectar and pollen feeders. Their tongues are long and covered in rows of papillae which increases the surface area and acts like a nectar absorbing mop. Pollen is a valuable food resource but is only eaten when they groom it from their fur and wings after nectar feeding. Microscopic examination of their fur reveals pollen-trapping scales on their hairs. In New South Wales they feed predominantly on Banksia and Callistemon nectar and pollen, no fruits or insects being eaten. In northern Queensland they eat Melaleuca, Banksia, Grevillea and Eucalyptus blossom as well as the nectar of many rainforest plants, particularly the cauliflorous Syzigium. These bats are good pollinators as they carry considerably more pollen than either birds or insects and it often flies directly between flowering trees. They will fly up to 6 km over cleared paddocks and may be a vital disperser of pollen to isolated fragments of tropical rainforest. They tend to eat more fruit, particularly figs, in the northern areas. Fig seeds particularly from Ficus variagata, F. congesta and F. racemoca as well as seeds of Timonius and Climbing Pandan, Freycineta spp are frequently found in the faeces.
       
They are opportunistic feeders and can appear in large numbers at a briefly flowering tree. Marked bats have returned to feeding sites over several years. They remain actively foraging for nearly half the night before returning to their day roost shortly before dawn. They are influenced by the brightness of the moon and bats will delay departure by up to 4 hours on strongly moonlit nights. They actively forage for most of the night and continue to forage during the rain.
