Inland Forest Bat
Vespadelus baverstocki

(Kitchener, Jones and Caputi, 1987)

Description. A very small bat. There are at least two distinct colour forms. In one, the fur on the back is light sandy brown with the base of the hairs grey. The belly fur is much lighter, the base of each hair is dark brown and the tip creamy white. The other colour form is brownish grey with a paler belly. The bare skin on the face is a greyish pink, with the ears and wing membranes pale grey. The tragus is sometimes white. The penis is pendulous and the end of the penis is clearly swollen. The head of the glans penis is funnel-shaped, without lateral urethral folds or a deep furrow on ventral surface and is not compressed. At the southern limit of its distribution (in north-western Victoria) V. baverstocki is significantly larger than elsewhere in its range.

Roost Habits Inland Forest Bats roost in tree hollows and abandoned buildings. There are no large trees over much of their distribution and they are known to roost in extremely small tree hollows in stunted trees only a few metres high. One large colony, of over 60, was found living in the crack around a door frame in an abandoned railway building.

Habitat. They are found in Acacia, Callitris and Casuarina woodlands, mallee, open eucalypt woodland, river red gum woodland, shrub and grassland communities across inland Australia.

Diet, Flight and Foraging. These are very manoeuvrable bats that fly with a rapid wing beat and forage below canopy height. They forage over a wide area. Their diet has not been studied.

Vocalisations and Echolocation. Characteristic frequency 39 to 46 kHz (n = 23*) Curved, usually with up-sweeping tail. Consecutive pulses even. Similar in frequency to Chalinolobus picatus but can be differentiated by lack of alternation. Similar in frequency to Vespadelus vulturnus in the north west (47 to 48 kHz) and in the Riverina (43 to 50 kHz).
